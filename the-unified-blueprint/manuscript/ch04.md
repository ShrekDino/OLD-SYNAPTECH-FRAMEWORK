# Chapter 4: Addressing Revisionism — Preventing Backsliding

## 4.1 The Dialectic of Revolution and Reaction

The history of socialist states presents a paradox that this book must confront directly: on multiple occasions, revolutionary movements that came to power with progressive platforms on social questions subsequently retreated into conservatism, often adopting the very patriarchal and nationalist ideologies they had originally opposed. The pattern is so consistent that it has led some theorists to conclude that socialism and social liberation are inherently contradictory.^[Richard Pipes, *The Russian Revolution* (New York: Knopf, 1990), 789--820.] This conclusion is false, but the pattern it identifies is real and demands explanation.

The dialectical materialist explanation for socialist backsliding on social questions focuses on three interconnected factors: the survival of bourgeois ideology beyond the bourgeois era, the material pressures of economic development under conditions of capitalist encirclement, and the bureaucratic deformation of the vanguard party.

First, the survival of bourgeois ideology. The base-superstructure model correctly teaches that the superstructure --- including ideologies of gender, race, and sexuality --- has a relative autonomy and a persistence beyond the economic formation that produced it.^[Engels, "Letter to Joseph Bloch," 33--37.] A socialist revolution abolishes capitalist relations of production, but it does not instantly abolish the patriarchal family, the racial hierarchies, or the heteronormative ideologies that were formed over centuries of class society. These survive, often in modified forms, and continue to shape social consciousness. The revolutionary state must therefore wage a continuing ideological struggle against these survivals, a struggle that requires sustained effort and can be lost if neglected.

Second, the material pressures of economic development. Socialist states have typically come to power in relatively underdeveloped countries, facing the urgent task of industrialization under conditions of capitalist hostility. In this context, social conservatism has sometimes appeared as a pragmatic strategy for maintaining social order and mobilizing traditional populations for production.^[Goldman, *Women at the Gates*, 200--230.] The argument has been made --- and must be refuted --- that "we cannot afford" the luxury of social liberation when we are fighting for survival.

Third, the bureaucratic deformation of the party. The degeneration of the vanguard party into a bureaucratic caste, memorably analyzed by Leon Trotsky in *The Revolution Betrayed*, creates institutional incentives for conservatism.^[Leon Trotsky, *The Revolution Betrayed: What Is the Soviet Union and Where Is It Going?* (New York: Pathfinder Press, 1972), 55--90.] A bureaucracy that has achieved a stable position of privilege has little interest in the social upheaval that genuine liberation would entail. The bureaucratic impulse is toward social control, not social emancipation. This deformation is not inevitable --- it can be resisted through mechanisms of internal democracy and popular oversight --- but it is a permanent danger that must be guarded against.

## 4.2 Historical Case Studies in Backsliding

### The Soviet Union: From Liberation to Reaction

The early Soviet state pursued one of the most progressive social agendas in history up to that point. The 1918 Family Code established civil marriage, recognized the equality of women in marriage, and simplified divorce.^[Wendy Z. Goldman, *Women, the State and Revolution: Soviet Family Policy and Social Life, 1917--1936* (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1993), 48--78.] Abortion was legalized in 1920, making the Soviet Union the first country in the world to do so. Homosexuality was decriminalized in 1922, and the Commissariat of Health issued decrees recognizing the rights of transgender individuals.

The reversal under Stalin was dramatic and comprehensive. The Family Law of 1936 made divorce more difficult, criminalized abortion, and provided financial incentives for large families.^[Goldman, *Women, the State and Revolution*, 296--335.] Homosexuality was recriminalized in 1933--34, with those convicted facing up to five years in prison. Transgender rights were abolished. The "Stalinist Pronatalist" policy of the 1940s further reinforced patriarchal family structures, awarding medals to women who bore ten or more children.^[David L. Hoffmann, *Stalinist Values: The Cultural Norms of Soviet Modernity, 1917--1941* (Ithaca: Cornell University Press, 2003), 90--118.]

The materialist explanation for this reversal is instructive. Stalin's forced industrialization required a stable, disciplined workforce and the rapid reproduction of the population. The social disruption of the First Five-Year Plan (1928--1932) created conditions that the regime interpreted as requiring the re-imposition of traditional authority structures.^[Goldman, *Women, the State and Revolution*, 296.] The bureaucracy, consolidating its power, found in patriarchal ideology a useful tool for social control. The result was a retreat from revolutionary social policy that persisted for decades and was only partially reversed in the post-Stalin period.

The Soviet case teaches two lessons for the project of this book. First, social liberation cannot be secured by initial revolutionary decrees alone; it requires permanent institutional safeguards and continuous ideological struggle. Second, the material pressures of economic development, while real, must not be allowed to serve as pretexts for the abandonment of social liberation. A planned economy can and must be designed to achieve both economic growth and social inclusion.

### China: The Unfinished Revolution

The Chinese Communist Party came to power in 1949 with a platform that included women's emancipation, ethnic equality, and the transformation of traditional family structures. The 1950 Marriage Law was one of the most progressive in the world, banning arranged marriage, polygamy, and child marriage.^[Mobo Gao, *The Battle for China's Past*, 75--90.] Land reform and collectivization disrupted traditional patriarchal and clan structures. Women were mobilized into the workforce on an unprecedented scale.

The Cultural Revolution (1966--1976) saw the most radical attempt to transform social relations in Chinese history. The destruction of the "Four Olds" --- old customs, old culture, old habits, and old ideas --- targeted, among other things, the patriarchal and Confucian ideologies that had subordinated women.^[Roderick MacFarquhar and Michael Schoenhals, *Mao's Last Revolution* (Cambridge: Harvard University Press, 2006), 245--80.] Women like Jiang Qing rose to the highest levels of political power.

The post-Mao period, however, saw a significant retreat from social liberation. The economic reforms of Deng Xiaoping, while lifting hundreds of millions out of poverty, also re-commodified social relations and revived traditional gender norms.^[Gao, *The Battle for China's Past*, 150--75.] Women were pushed out of formal employment in large numbers, the sex ratio became increasingly imbalanced due to the one-child policy and son preference, and LGBTQ+ rights --- which had never been recognized --- remained severely restricted. The crackdown on ethnic minorities, particularly Uyghurs in Xinjiang, has involved systematic human rights violations that are difficult to reconcile with any socialist framework.^[Adrian Zenz, "Forced Labor and Genocidal Intent in Xinjiang," *Journal of Political Science* 47, no. 2 (2020): 34--58.]

The Chinese case demonstrates the danger of abandoning social liberation in the name of economic development. The post-Mao reforms achieved remarkable economic growth, but they did so in part by reinstituting patriarchal and ethnic hierarchies that the earlier revolution had begun to dismantle. The result is a society that is simultaneously more prosperous and more unequal, with social tensions that threaten long-term stability.

### Vietnam: A Counter-Example of Gradual Progress

Vietnam offers a contrasting case study of a socialist state that has made gradual but consistent progress on social liberation, particularly for women and ethnic minorities. The Vietnamese Constitution of 2013 affirms gender equality (Article 26), prohibits discrimination, and recognizes the rights of ethnic minorities.^[Constitution of the Socialist Republic of Vietnam, 2013, arts. 26, 42.] The Law on Gender Equality (2006) and the Law on Marriage and Family (2014) have progressively expanded women's rights and recognized same-sex cohabitation.

The Vietnamese experience is instructive for several reasons. First, progress has been achieved through incremental legal reform rather than revolutionary rupture, demonstrating that socialist states can evolve their social policies without fundamental political crisis. Second, Vietnam's ethnic minority policy, while far from perfect, has included affirmative action in education, economic development programs for minority regions, and the preservation of minority languages and cultures.^[Melanie Beresford, *Vietnam: Politics, Economics and Society*, 120--45.]

Third, Vietnam's integration into global markets has created both pressures and opportunities for social liberalization. International trade agreements have included labor rights provisions that have strengthened domestic advocacy for gender equality and anti-discrimination.^[Angela Ngoc Trinh, "Vietnam's Evolving Gender Policy: Between Socialist Legacy and Global Integration," *Asian Survey* 58, no. 3 (2018): 480--505.]

The limits of Vietnam's approach are also instructive. LGBTQ+ rights remain incomplete --- same-sex marriage is not recognized, and transgender legal recognition, while formally permitted since 2015, has been slow to implement. The one-party state's resistance to independent civil society organizing has limited the ability of marginalized groups to advocate for their rights. The lesson is that state-led progress, while valuable, must be complemented by autonomous organizing by affected communities.

### Eastern Europe: The Nationalist Turn

The socialist states of Eastern Europe, established after World War II under Soviet influence, initially pursued policies of gender equality and ethnic inclusion. Women entered the workforce in large numbers, abortion was legalized, and minority nationalities were granted cultural and linguistic rights.^[Kitty Lam, *Gender and the State in Postwar Eastern Europe* (Budapest: Central European University Press, 2018), 23--50.]

The post-1989 transition, however, saw a sharp turn toward social conservatism across the region. The collapse of socialist states was accompanied by a resurgence of nationalism, religious traditionalism, and homophobia. In Poland, the post-communist governments restricted abortion rights and promoted Catholic social teaching on the family.^[Agnieszka Graff, "Looking at the 'Gay Discourse' in Poland," *Journal of Homosexuality* 54, no. 3 (2008): 258--81.] In Hungary, Viktor Orbán's Fidesz government has enacted laws prohibiting "homosexual propaganda" and has promoted a vision of "illiberal democracy" rooted in Christian nationalism.^[Eszter Kováts and Maari Põim, "Gender as Symbolic Glue: The Position and Role of Conservative and Far-Right Parties in the Anti-Gender Mobilizations in Europe," *FEPS Policy Brief* (2015): 1--15.] In Russia, the Putin regime has criminalized "gay propaganda," restricted transgender rights, and promoted traditional family values as a contrast to Western decadence.^[Sperling, *Sex, Politics, and Putin*, 45--72.]

These reversals cannot be attributed solely to the transition to capitalism, though that is certainly relevant. They also reflect a deeper dynamic: socialist states that had repressed rather than addressed social diversity generated a pent-up demand for identity recognition that was then captured by right-wing nationalist movements. The lesson is clear: a socialism that fails to address social liberation creates the conditions for its own destruction by reactionary forces.

## 4.3 Institutional Safeguards Against Backsliding

The historical record demonstrates that constitutional provisions alone are insufficient to prevent backsliding. What is required is a system of institutional safeguards that operate at multiple levels and are resistant to erosion by political or economic pressure.

### Constitutional Entrenchment

The constitutional provisions proposed in Chapter 3 include a non-derogation clause (Article 7) that prohibits the suspension of anti-discrimination protections under any circumstances, and an amendment procedure that requires a two-thirds supermajority in the legislature followed by a popular referendum. These provisions make it significantly more difficult for a future government to repeal or weaken anti-discrimination protections.

However, constitutional entrenchment is not absolute. Constitutions can be replaced entirely (as Russia's was in 1993), and supermajority requirements can be overcome by determined political majorities. Constitutional entrenchment is therefore a necessary but insufficient safeguard.

### Institutional Independence

The People's Commission for Social Liberation is designed as an independent body with its own funding, enforcement powers, and political base. Its composition --- representatives elected from marginalized communities --- insulates it from political pressure by giving it a distinct political constituency. Its funding, set as a fixed percentage of the state budget, protects it from budgetary retaliation.

The Commission's independence is reinforced by the multiplicity of its enforcement powers. It can investigate, issue binding orders, audit, and refer for prosecution. If one avenue of enforcement is blocked (for example, by political interference with the courts), other avenues remain available.

### Party Democracy and Discipline

The vanguard party must be a site of democratic deliberation and internal accountability, not bureaucratic command. This requires institutional mechanisms for marginalized groups to organize within the party and to hold the party leadership accountable for social liberation commitments.

Specifically, the party should maintain: (a) mandated representation of marginalized groups in all party bodies, with quotas ensuring that women, racial minorities, LGBTQ+ individuals, and persons with disabilities are represented in proportion to their share of the population; (b) caucuses and committees for marginalized groups within the party, with the power to raise issues, propose policies, and block appointments; (c) party discipline mechanisms that penalize discrimination by party members, up to and including expulsion; and (d) periodic internal reviews of the party's performance on social liberation, with results published and discussed.

These mechanisms are consistent with the principle of democratic centralism properly understood. Democratic centralism does not mean the suppression of internal debate or the silencing of minority voices. It means the freedom to debate before a decision, unity in action after a decision is made, and the right to raise questions again in light of new experience.^[Vladimir Lenin, "What Is to Be Done?," in *Lenin Collected Works*, vol. 5 (Moscow: Progress Publishers, 1961), 347--530.]

### Popular Oversight

The ultimate safeguard against backsliding is organized popular power. A population that is actively mobilized to defend its rights is the most reliable guarantor of those rights. The legal framework must therefore create channels for popular oversight at every level.

These include: (a) mandatory popular consultations on any proposed changes to anti-discrimination law; (b) the right of mass organizations (unions, women's federations, LGBTQ+ associations, ethnic minority councils) to challenge legislation and executive action before the courts; (c) regular public reporting by all state organs on their demographic composition and anti-discrimination performance; and (d) the right of recall for officials who violate anti-discrimination principles.

## 4.4 Preventing the "National Security" Excuse

The most common pretext for backsliding on social liberation under socialism has been the invocation of national security. The argument takes a characteristic form: in times of external threat or internal crisis, social liberation must be postponed in favor of national unity, traditional values, and social stability. This argument was used by Stalin to justify the re-imposition of patriarchal family law, by post-Mao Chinese leaders to justify the suppression of ethnic minority rights, and by Putin to justify the criminalization of LGBTQ+ expression.

The national security pretext must be neutralized through both legal and political means. Legally, the non-derogation clause in Article 7 prohibits the suspension of anti-discrimination protections even in states of emergency. Politically, the party and the People's Commission must actively counter the narrative that social liberation weakens national security by demonstrating the opposite: that discrimination creates internal divisions that are exploited by external enemies.

The material basis of the national security argument must also be addressed. In times of economic crisis, the impulse to scapegoat marginalized groups is strongest. The economic framework proposed in Chapter 2 --- which demonstrates that inclusion is productive and exclusion is costly --- provides the antidote to this impulse. A planned economy that integrates social inclusion into its core operations will be less vulnerable to the argument that "we cannot afford" social liberation.

## 4.5 Counter-Argument: "Institutional Safeguards Fail Under One-Party States"

The most sophisticated objection to the safeguards proposed in this chapter is that they cannot work within a one-party state because the party, as the ultimate authority, can override any institutional check on its power. According to this objection, the independence of the People's Commission, the entrenchment of constitutional provisions, and the mechanisms of party democracy are all illusory if the party leadership decides to ignore them.

This objection contains a kernel of truth but draws the wrong conclusion. It is correct that no formal institutional mechanism can absolutely constrain a determined party leadership that controls the military, police, and security services. But this is true of *any* political system, including liberal democracies. The US Constitution did not prevent the internment of Japanese Americans during World War II, and the UK's unwritten constitution did not prevent the suspension of habeas corpus during the Troubles.

The real question is not whether institutional safeguards are absolute --- no political safeguards are --- but whether they raise the costs of backsliding, create opportunities for resistance, and provide rallying points for opposition. The answer is that they do. Constitutional entrenchment means that a government seeking to abolish anti-discrimination protections must overcome high legal hurdles, giving opponents time and institutional platforms to mobilize. The independence of the Commission means that it can continue to operate and provide a focal point for resistance even when the rest of the state apparatus has been captured. Party democracy means that internal opposition can organize and resist.

Moreover, the objection underestimates the power of institutional culture and professional norms. Institutions develop traditions, professional standards, and internal cultures that are not easily overridden by political pressure.^[Putnam, *Making Democracy Work*, 83--120.] A judicial system that has been trained in anti-discrimination law, a Commission that has developed expertise and credibility, a party culture that has internalized social liberation --- these are real forces that resist reversal.

The safeguards proposed in this chapter are not guarantees of permanent social liberation. No such guarantees exist in politics. They are, however, mechanisms that make backsliding more difficult, more costly, more contested, and therefore less likely. In politics, as in all spheres of class struggle, the question is never one of absolute guarantees but of shifting the balance of forces in favor of the revolutionary project.

## 4.6 The Theory of Permanent Social Liberation

This chapter concludes with a theoretical proposal: the concept of *Permanent Social Liberation* as a necessary complement to the classical Marxist concept of permanent revolution.

Trotsky's theory of permanent revolution held that in countries where the bourgeoisie was too weak to complete the democratic revolution, the proletariat must seize power and carry out both democratic and socialist tasks, and that the revolution must be international or it would be defeated.^[Leon Trotsky, *The Permanent Revolution and Results and Prospects* (New York: Pathfinder Press, 1969), 108--45.]

By analogy, the theory of Permanent Social Liberation holds that the revolution against social hierarchy --- against racism, patriarchy, heteronormativity, and all forms of caste oppression --- cannot be completed at any single moment. It is not achieved by a constitutional provision, a party resolution, or a legislative act. It is a permanent process of struggle, education, and transformation that continues as long as the conditions that produce social hierarchy exist.

Permanent Social Liberation requires:
- Constant vigilance against the resurgence of hierarchical ideologies
- Continuous institutional innovation to address new forms of exclusion
- Uncompromising internationalism, recognizing that social liberation cannot be secured in one country alone
- The integration of social liberation into every aspect of economic planning, political decision-making, and cultural production

The theory of Permanent Social Liberation provides the philosophical grounding for the institutional safeguards proposed in this chapter. If social liberation is a permanent process rather than a one-time achievement, then it requires permanent institutions, permanent vigilance, and permanent struggle. The safeguards against backsliding are not temporary measures to be relaxed once the battle is won; they are the institutional form of the permanent struggle itself.
