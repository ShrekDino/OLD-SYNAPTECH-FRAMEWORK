# Chapter 3: The Legal Framework — Constitutionalizing Liberation

## 3.1 Principles of Socialist Constitutionalism

Law under socialism has a fundamentally different character from law under capitalism. The bourgeois legal order presents itself as neutral, universal, and above class conflict, when in fact it functions to legitimate and reproduce class domination. As Evgeny Pashukanis argued in *The General Theory of Law and Marxism*, the legal form itself is a bourgeois form, arising from the commodity exchange relations of capitalism.^[Evgeny Pashukanis, *The General Theory of Law and Marxism*, trans. Barbara Einhorn (New Brunswick: Transaction Books, 2002), 109--27.] Under socialism, law must be transformed from an instrument of class rule into an instrument of class emancipation.

This transformation does not mean the abolition of law altogether (a position associated with the early Soviet "withering away of law" thesis, which was never fully implemented and has been largely abandoned), but rather its systematic repurposing. Socialist law retains coercive power, but it directs that power against the enemies of the revolutionary order and in defense of the revolutionary transformation of social relations. As P.I. Stuchka, the first Soviet Minister of Justice, put it: "Law is a system of social relations corresponding to the interests of the ruling class."^[P.I. Stuchka, *Selected Writings on Soviet Law and Marxism*, ed. Robert Sharlet (Armonk: M.E. Sharpe, 1988), 105.]

The extension of this principle to social liberation is straightforward. If the ruling class under socialism is the proletariat (exercising its dictatorship through the vanguard party and state apparatus), then the law must correspond to the interests of the proletariat in its full diversity. A legal system that protects racial hierarchies or heteronormative structures is, by definition, not serving proletarian interests --- it is serving the interests of a fraction of the proletariat and its allies at the expense of others. Such a system is not socialist law; it is bourgeois law operating under a socialist label.

The legal framework proposed in this chapter therefore rests on three principles:

1. **Anti-Discrimination as a Constitutional Imperative**: The prohibition of discrimination based on race, ethnicity, sexual orientation, gender identity, sex characteristics, and caste must be enshrined in the constitution itself, not merely in ordinary legislation. This entrenchment ensures that anti-discrimination norms cannot be modified by simple legislative majorities and forms the legal basis for all subordinate legislation and administrative action.

2. **Positive State Obligation**: The state has an affirmative duty not merely to refrain from discrimination but actively to prevent discrimination by private actors, to remedy historical patterns of exclusion, and to create the material conditions for genuine equality. This positive obligation distinguishes socialist anti-discrimination law from the liberal model, which typically prohibits state discrimination but imposes weaker duties regarding private action and structural inequality.

3. **Counter-Revolutionary Status**: Severe and systematic discrimination constitutes a counter-revolutionary act --- that is, an act that undermines the unity of the people and destabilizes the socialist order. This designation justifies enhanced penalties and expanded enforcement powers while making clear that anti-discrimination is not a secondary concern but a core element of socialist legality.

## 3.2 Draft Constitutional Provisions

The following constitutional articles are proposed as a model for integration into the fundamental law of a socialist state. They are drafted in a style appropriate for a written constitution, with sufficient specificity to be self-executing while leaving room for legislative elaboration.

---

### Article 1: Principle of Unity in Diversity

1. The socialist state is founded on the unity of the working people in their full diversity. This diversity includes, without limitation, differences of race, ethnicity, nationality, language, religion, sex, gender, gender identity, sexual orientation, sex characteristics, age, disability, and caste.

2. The unity of the people is strengthened, not weakened, by the recognition and protection of diversity. Discrimination that divides the people is a contradiction within the revolutionary order and shall be treated as such.

3. The state has the affirmative obligation to promote understanding, respect, and solidarity among all groups within the population and to combat ideologies and practices that incite division or hatred.

---

### Article 2: Prohibition of Discrimination

1. No person shall be discriminated against by the state, any state organ, any enterprise, any organization, or any private person on grounds of race, ethnicity, nationality, language, religion, sex, gender, gender identity, sexual orientation, sex characteristics, age, disability, or caste.

2. Discrimination includes: (a) direct differential treatment that imposes a burden or denies a benefit on a prohibited ground; (b) indirect discrimination through the application of apparently neutral criteria that have a disproportionate adverse effect on members of a protected group; (c) harassment, intimidation, or violence motivated by prejudice against a protected group; and (d) failure to provide reasonable accommodation for the specific needs of protected groups.

3. The state shall enact legislation defining the specific forms of discrimination, establishing enforcement mechanisms, and providing for remedies including injunctive relief, compensation, and criminal penalties.

---

### Article 3: Equality of Access

1. Access to the means of production, employment, housing, healthcare, education, culture, transportation, and all other goods and services provided or regulated by the state shall be guaranteed without discrimination on any of the grounds listed in Article 2(1).

2. The state shall take affirmative measures to eliminate historical patterns of exclusion and to ensure the equitable representation of all groups in all sectors of social and economic life.

3. Such affirmative measures shall not themselves constitute prohibited discrimination and shall be subject to periodic review and renewal based on the continuing existence of material inequality.

---

### Article 4: People's Commission for Social Liberation

1. There shall be an independent constitutional commission known as the People's Commission for Social Liberation, composed of representatives elected by the population from among members of historically marginalized groups.

2. The Commission shall have the power to: (a) investigate allegations of discrimination and systemic exclusion; (b) issue binding orders to state organs, enterprises, and individuals to cease discriminatory practices and remedy their effects; (c) recommend legislation and policy changes to the legislative and executive authorities; (d) audit state organs and enterprises for compliance with anti-discrimination obligations; and (e) initiate legal proceedings, including criminal prosecutions, for violations of this chapter.

3. The Commission shall report annually to the highest legislative body and its reports shall be public.

4. Decisions of the Commission may be appealed to the Supreme People's Court on questions of law.

---

### Article 5: Hate Speech and Incitement

1. The incitement to discrimination, hatred, or violence against any person or group on any of the grounds listed in Article 2(1) is prohibited.

2. The dissemination of ideologies that justify or promote the superiority of any group over others on racial, ethnic, religious, or other grounds is prohibited.

3. The prohibition in paragraphs (1) and (2) shall be implemented through legislation that respects the freedom of scientific inquiry and artistic expression while protecting the equality and dignity of all persons.

4. The state shall maintain a program of public education to combat prejudice and promote the principles of equality and solidarity.

---

### Article 6: Discrimination as a Counter-Revolutionary Act

1. Systematic discrimination that causes severe harm to individuals or groups, that is committed by persons holding state authority, or that is organized as a pattern of resistance to the anti-discrimination provisions of this Constitution, shall be classified as a counter-revolutionary act.

2. The classification in paragraph (1) shall not apply to isolated acts of discrimination by individual citizens not holding state authority, which shall be addressed through ordinary civil and criminal procedures.

3. Counter-revolutionary discrimination shall be investigated by the relevant state security organs and tried before the appropriate courts, with all procedural guarantees required by law.

4. This article shall be implemented in a manner consistent with the principle of proportionality, ensuring that the severity of punishment corresponds to the gravity of the offense.

---

### Article 7: Non-Derogation

1. The rights and protections established in this chapter shall not be suspended or limited under any circumstances, including states of emergency, war, or threats to national security.

2. Any legislation, executive action, or judicial decision that violates this chapter shall be null and void.

3. Amendments to this chapter shall require a two-thirds supermajority vote in the highest legislative body, followed by ratification through a popular referendum.

---

### Article 8: International Solidarity

1. The socialist state shall extend its anti-discrimination principles to its foreign policy, including trade agreements, diplomatic relations, and international assistance programs.

2. The state shall grant asylum to individuals fleeing persecution on any of the grounds listed in Article 2(1).

3. The state shall cooperate with other states and international organizations to combat discrimination and promote social liberation globally.

---

## 3.3 Comparative Constitutional Analysis

The proposed framework draws on lessons from multiple constitutional traditions, each of which offers insights and warnings for socialist constitution-making.

### South Africa's Equality Model

The Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996, contains one of the world's most comprehensive equality provisions. Section 9 prohibits discrimination on nineteen grounds, including race, gender, sex, pregnancy, marital status, ethnic or social origin, color, sexual orientation, age, disability, religion, conscience, belief, culture, language, and birth.^[Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996, sec. 9.] The South African Constitutional Court has developed a robust jurisprudence of equality, including a distinction between formal and substantive equality that has informed the positive obligation model adopted in this chapter.

South Africa's experience offers three crucial lessons for socialist constitution-making. First, constitutional equality provisions, however well-drafted, are insufficient without material transformation. Despite its exemplary constitutional framework, South Africa remains one of the world's most unequal societies, and the gap between constitutional promise and material reality has fueled political instability and resurgent racial tensions.^[Patrick Bond, *Elite Transition: From Apartheid to Neoliberalism in South Africa* (London: Pluto Press, 2014), 15--40.] The lesson is that constitutional equality must be accompanied by economic transformation, including the decommodification of essential goods and services and the redistribution of productive assets.

Second, the South African experience demonstrates the importance of independent enforcement institutions. The South African Human Rights Commission, established under Section 184 of the Constitution, has played a significant role in investigating violations and promoting human rights culture. However, its effectiveness has been limited by resource constraints and political interference.^[Human Rights Watch, *South Africa: Human Rights Commission Underfunded, Overwhelmed* (New York: Human Rights Watch, 2019).] The People's Commission for Social Liberation proposed in this chapter addresses these limitations by constitutionalizing its funding, granting it binding enforcement powers, and ensuring its composition reflects the communities it serves.

Third, South Africa shows the danger of constitutional provisions without popular mobilization. The Constitution's equality provisions were the product of a mass democratic movement and reflect the aspirations of that movement. But as the movement has fragmented, the Constitution has become increasingly embattled, and the equality provisions have faced sustained political attack. Constitutional protections are not self-enforcing; they require organized popular defense.^[Richard Calland, *The Zuma Years: South Africa's Changing Face of Power* (Cape Town: Zebra Press, 2013), 90--115.]

### Cuba's Socialist Constitutionalism

Cuba's 2019 Constitution represents the most recent attempt to integrate social liberation into a socialist constitutional framework. The Constitution explicitly prohibits discrimination based on race, sex, gender identity, sexual orientation, and disability (Article 42).^[Constitution of the Republic of Cuba, 2019, art. 42.] It recognizes same-sex marriage (Article 82), protects gender identity, and establishes the state's obligation to promote equality.

Cuba's experience is particularly instructive for the project of this book because it demonstrates both the possibilities and the tensions of socialist constitutionalism on social questions. The 2019 Constitution was preceded by an extraordinary public consultation process in which millions of Cubans participated in discussing the draft.^[August Nimtz, "Cuba's 2019 Constitution: Continuity and Change," *Socialism and Democracy* 33, no. 2 (2019): 1--19.] The inclusion of LGBTQIA+ protections was the result of decades of activism by Cuban queer organizations, building on the work of figures like Mariela Castro and the National Center for Sex Education.

Yet the Cuban process also reveals the limits of constitutional reform without corresponding cultural and institutional transformation. Despite the Constitution's progressive provisions, implementation has been uneven. Transgender Cubans continue to face discrimination in employment and social life.^[Emily J. Kirk, "Cuba's LGBTQ+ Community: Progress and Challenges," *International Journal of Cuban Studies* 12, no. 1 (2020): 88--108.] The tension between the Constitution's egalitarian promise and the persistence of social hierarchy underscores the need for the comprehensive institutional architecture --- including the People's Commission for Social Liberation --- proposed in this chapter.

### Brazil's Juridical Progressivism

Brazil offers a distinctive model of LGBTQ+ and racial rights advancement through judicial interpretation within a framework that remains capitalist and deeply unequal. The Brazilian Constitution of 1988 prohibits discrimination on grounds of race, sex, and "any other form of prejudice" (Article 3, IV),^[Constituição da República Federativa do Brasil, 1988, art. 3, IV.] and the Supreme Federal Court (STF) has played an increasingly active role in expanding protections for marginalized groups.

In ADI 4275 (2019), the STF ruled that transgender individuals have the right to change their name and gender on official documents without requiring surgery, judicial authorization, or medical evaluation.^[Supremo Tribunal Federal, ADI 4275, Relator: Min. Marco Aurélio, julgado em 1º de março de 2019.] In ADPF 132 (2011), the Court recognized same-sex civil unions, and in ADO 26 (2019), it criminalized homophobia and transphobia, classifying them as forms of racism.^[Supremo Tribunal Federal, ADPF 132, Relator: Min. Ayres Britto, julgado em 5 de maio de 2011; Supremo Tribunal Federal, ADO 26, Relator: Min. Celso de Mello, julgado em 13 de junho de 2019.] The STF's decision in ADO 26 is particularly significant for the project of this book, as it treats discrimination based on sexual orientation and gender identity as analogous to racial discrimination and subjects it to the same legal regime --- including criminal penalties.

Brazil's racial equality framework has also evolved through both legislation and judicial action. The Statute of Racial Equality (Law No. 12,288/2010) establishes affirmative action policies, and the STF has upheld the constitutionality of racial quotas in public universities.^[Lei No. 12.288, de 20 de Julho de 2010 (Estatuto da Igualdade Racial); STF, ADPF 186, Relator: Min. Ricardo Lewandowski, julgado em 26 de abril de 2012.]

Brazil's experience offers three lessons for socialist constitution-making. First, judicial progressivism can advance social rights even in the absence of legislative action --- but judicial victories are vulnerable to political backlash and institutional capture. The Bolsonaro government (2019--2022) systematically sought to undermine LGBTQ+ and racial equality protections, demonstrating the fragility of judge-made rights.^[James N. Green, "Brazil's LGBTQ+ Movement Under Bolsonaro," *NACLA Report on the Americas* 53, no. 2 (2021): 145--52.]

Second, constitutional protections without corresponding material transformation leave marginalized groups vulnerable. Brazil's progressive jurisprudence has not prevented high rates of violence against trans people --- Brazil remains the country with the highest rate of transgender murders globally.^[Transgender Europe, *Trans Murder Monitoring 2022* (Berlin: TGEU, 2022).] The gap between legal recognition and material safety underscores the need for the comprehensive institutional framework proposed in this chapter.

Third, Brazil demonstrates that anti-discrimination law must be actively enforced to have effect. The criminalization of homophobia in ADO 26 has had limited practical impact because of inadequate police training, prosecutorial reluctance, and judicial bias. The institutional mechanisms proposed in this chapter --- including mandatory training, independent enforcement, and popular oversight --- are designed to address the implementation gap that Brazil's experience reveals.

### The Soviet Nationality Policy

The early Soviet nationality policy, particularly the policy of *korenizatsiya* (indigenization), offers a historical model of affirmative state action to address ethnic hierarchy. Under this policy, the Soviet state promoted the languages, cultures, and political leadership of non-Russian nationalities, creating national territories with their own schools, media, and administrative institutions.^[Terry Martin, *The Affirmative Action Empire*, 15--31.]

The achievements of this policy were substantial. Literacy rates among previously colonized peoples rose dramatically. National intelligentsias were formed. The political representation of ethnic minorities increased significantly. These achievements demonstrate that a socialist state can, through conscious and sustained intervention, reduce ethnic hierarchy and promote the inclusion of marginalized groups.

However, the policy also had significant limitations that inform the legal framework proposed here. First, the policy's benefits were distributed unevenly, with larger nationalities benefiting more than smaller ones. Second, the policy was vulnerable to reversal: Stalin's Great Retreat in the 1930s saw the purging of minority cadres, the closure of national institutions, and the reassertion of Russian dominance.^[Martin, *The Affirmative Action Empire*, 342--78.] Third, the policy operated within a framework that treated nationality as a fixed category rather than recognizing the fluidity and intersectionality of identity.

The legal framework proposed in this chapter addresses these limitations through constitutional entrenchment (preventing reversal through ordinary political processes), intersectional design (recognizing that individuals hold multiple identities that require simultaneous protection), and independent enforcement (insulating anti-discrimination institutions from political pressure).

## 3.4 Counter-Argument: "Woke Authoritarianism"

The most politically charged objection to the legal framework proposed in this chapter is that it represents a form of "woke authoritarianism" --- the use of state power to enforce a particular ideology of social liberation, suppressing dissent and punishing those who hold traditional views. This objection comes from both the right (which defends traditional values against progressive encroachment) and the libertarian left (which is skeptical of state power in any form).

This objection must be addressed seriously, not dismissed, because it touches on genuine questions about the relationship between law, ideology, and coercion in a socialist society.

First, the objection conflates the suppression of discrimination with the suppression of dissent. The legal framework proposed in this chapter does not criminalize holding prejudiced beliefs or belonging to religious traditions that teach traditional views on gender and sexuality. It criminalizes *acts of discrimination* that harm others --- refusing employment, housing, or services; subjecting people to harassment or violence; inciting hatred that leads to harm. The distinction between belief and action is fundamental to socialist legality.^[Pashukanis, *General Theory of Law and Marxism*, 150--70.]

Second, the objection ignores the coercive character of existing social relations. The claim that anti-discrimination law represents an unprecedented intrusion of state power into private life is made possible only by rendering invisible the existing coercion embedded in social norms. A transgender person who cannot access healthcare, a same-sex couple who cannot adopt children, a Black family who cannot buy a home in a particular neighborhood --- these are not "free choices" but forms of coercion produced and enforced by existing legal and social structures. Anti-discrimination law does not introduce coercion where none existed; it redirects coercion from the oppressed to the oppressor. As Lenin wrote, the state "is a special organization of force; it is an organization of violence for the suppression of some class."^[Vladimir Lenin, *The State and Revolution*, in *Lenin Collected Works*, vol. 25 (Moscow: Progress Publishers, 1974), 383.] Under socialism, this force must be wielded against the enemies of equality, not its defenders.

Third, the objection assumes a liberal model of freedom --- freedom as the absence of state constraint --- that is inadequate for a socialist society. Socialist freedom is not merely negative (freedom *from* interference) but positive (freedom *to* participate, to develop one's capacities, to contribute to social life).^[Marx, *Grundrisse*, 409--10.] The legal framework proposed in this chapter expands freedom by removing the barriers that prevent marginalized groups from participating fully in social and economic life. The "restriction" on discrimination is necessary condition for the expansion of freedom.

Finally, the objection underestimates the capacity of ordinary people to accept and internalize anti-discrimination norms when they are properly explained and implemented. The experience of countries that have implemented strong anti-discrimination laws --- South Africa, Canada, the Nordic countries --- is that public acceptance of such laws tends to increase over time as their benefits become apparent.^[Robert D. Putnam, *Making Democracy Work: Civic Traditions in Modern Italy* (Princeton: Princeton University Press, 1993), 83--120.] The initial resistance to anti-discrimination norms is not a permanent feature of human psychology but a reflection of the period of transition, when old prejudices persist alongside new legal structures. With consistent enforcement and education, these norms become internalized and the coercive apparatus can recede.

## 3.5 Enforcement Mechanisms

The constitutional framework proposed in this chapter requires robust enforcement mechanisms to be effective. These mechanisms operate at three levels: administrative, judicial, and popular.

### Administrative Enforcement

The People's Commission for Social Liberation serves as the primary administrative enforcement body. Its powers --- investigation, binding orders, audit, and prosecution referral --- are designed to allow it to address discrimination at multiple levels, from individual complaints to systemic patterns.

The Commission shall operate through a system of regional offices with their own investigative capacity and enforcement powers. Each office shall include specialized units dealing with race/ethnicity, gender/sexuality, disability, and caste/analogous hierarchies. These units shall employ investigators with relevant expertise and shall maintain relationships with community organizations to facilitate reporting and investigation.

The Commission's audit function is particularly important for systemic enforcement. Each year, the Commission shall audit a representative sample of state organs and enterprises, examining their demographic composition, hiring and promotion practices, and compliance with anti-discrimination obligations. The results of these audits shall be published and shall form the basis for recommendations and enforcement actions.

### Judicial Enforcement

Courts at all levels shall have jurisdiction over discrimination cases. Specialized anti-discrimination chambers within the people's court system shall be established to ensure expertise and consistency in adjudication. These chambers shall include judges with training in anti-discrimination law and shall be composed to reflect the diversity of the population.

The standard of proof in discrimination cases shall follow the burden-shifting framework developed in South African and Canadian jurisprudence: the complainant must establish a prima facie case of discrimination (showing differential treatment and a prohibited ground), whereupon the burden shifts to the respondent to show that the treatment was justified or non-discriminatory.^[*Ontario Human Rights Commission v. Simpsons-Sears*, [1985] 2 S.C.R. 536 (Supreme Court of Canada).]

Remedies for discrimination shall include: (a) injunctive relief ordering the cessation of discriminatory practices and the implementation of corrective measures; (b) compensation for economic losses, including lost wages and benefits; (c) damages for emotional distress and loss of dignity; (d) punitive damages for knowing or reckless violations; (e) orders for systemic reform, including the adoption of affirmative action plans; and (f) criminal penalties for hate crimes and systematic discrimination.

### Popular Enforcement

The ultimate guarantee of the legal framework is popular mobilization. Constitutional protections that lack organized popular support will be eroded or abolished, regardless of their formal legal status. The legal framework must therefore create channels for popular participation in enforcement and for the self-organization of marginalized communities.

The People's Commission for Social Liberation shall include mechanisms for popular participation at every level. Community advisory boards, composed of representatives from affected communities, shall advise the Commission on its priorities and evaluate its performance. Public hearings shall be held on systemic discrimination issues. Whistleblower protections shall encourage reporting of discrimination within state organs and enterprises. Legal aid shall be provided to complainants who lack resources to pursue their cases.

The party, as the vanguard of the working class, has a particular responsibility for popular enforcement. Party organizations at every level shall include officers responsible for monitoring discrimination and promoting inclusion. Party discipline shall be applied to members who engage in discrimination or fail to combat it. The party's internal democracy shall include mechanisms for marginalized groups to raise concerns and demand action.
