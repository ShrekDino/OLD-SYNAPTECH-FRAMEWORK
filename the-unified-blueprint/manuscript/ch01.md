# Chapter 1: The Philosophical Shift — From Class-Reductionism to Intersectional Materialism

## 1.1 The Classical Marxist Position on Social Identity

The classical Marxist tradition contains within itself both the resources for a robust theory of social liberation and the seeds of a reductionism that has historically marginalized such questions. To understand this duality is to grasp why a theoretical reconstruction is necessary and why it is faithful rather than hostile to the dialectical method.

Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels wrote relatively little about what we would today call identity-based oppression, but what they did write is revealing. In *The German Ideology*, Marx and Engels established that "the first premise of all human history is, of course, the existence of living human individuals."^[Karl Marx and Friedrich Engels, *The German Ideology*, in *Marx-Engels Collected Works*, vol. 5 (Moscow: Progress Publishers, 1976), 31.] This seemingly banal observation contains a profound methodological implication: any materialism that abstracts away from the concrete, embodied reality of human beings --- their sex, their race, their social position within hierarchies that predate capitalism --- has failed its own premises. Marx's analysis of primitive accumulation in *Capital*, Volume I, documented how the enclosure movements in England created a racialized and gendered proletariat, with women and Irish workers subjected to super-exploitation.^[Karl Marx, *Capital*, vol. 1, trans. Ben Fowkes (London: Penguin, 1976), 873--940.] The famous observation that "labor in the white skin can never free itself while labor in the black skin is branded"^[Marx, *Capital*, 1:414.] is not an incidental remark --- it is a structural insight that links racial oppression directly to the reproduction of capitalist social relations.

Engels's *The Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State* attempted a materialist account of women's subordination, tracing it to the emergence of class society and the transformation of the family into a unit of property transmission.^[Friedrich Engels, *The Origin of the Family, Private Property and the State*, in *Marx-Engels Collected Works*, vol. 26 (Moscow: Progress Publishers, 1990).] Though flawed by the anthropological limitations of its era, the work established a crucial principle: that gender hierarchy is not natural or eternal but has a historical material origin and is therefore susceptible to revolutionary transformation. Similarly, Engels's letters on Ireland developed an early materialist analysis of national and racial oppression, arguing that English capitalists deliberately cultivated anti-Irish racism to divide the working class.^[Friedrich Engels, "Letters on Ireland," in *Marx-Engels Collected Works*, vol. 43 (Moscow: Progress Publishers, 1988), 475--80.]

Vladimir Lenin and Joseph Stalin developed these insights further in their work on the national question. Lenin's *The Right of Nations to Self-Determination* argued that recognizing and fighting for national equality was not a concession to bourgeois nationalism but a necessary condition for international working-class unity.^[Vladimir Lenin, *The Right of Nations to Self-Determination*, in *Lenin Collected Works*, vol. 20 (Moscow: Progress Publishers, 1972), 393--454.] Stalin's *Marxism and the National Question*, whatever its subsequent deformations in practice, provided a materialist definition of nationhood that rejected both racial essentialism and bourgeois cosmopolitanism.^[Joseph Stalin, *Marxism and the National Question* (Moscow: Foreign Languages Publishing House, 1954).] The early Soviet experiment in *korenizatsiya* (indigenization) --- promoting native-language education and local cadre development among hundreds of ethnic groups --- represented the most ambitious state-led anti-racist project in history up to that point.^[Terry Martin, *The Affirmative Action Empire: Nations and Nationalism in the Soviet Union, 1923--1939* (Ithaca: Cornell University Press, 2001), 15--31.]

Yet for all these resources, classical Marxism developed a theoretical framework that systematically under-theorized race, gender, and sexuality as independent axes of oppression. The reasons for this are themselves materialist: Marx and Engels were writing in a context where industrial class conflict appeared as the dominant contradiction; the liberation movements of colonized peoples, women, and sexual minorities had not yet achieved the organizational maturity that would later force theoretical innovation. The Second International and much of the Third International treated the "woman question" and the "national question" as subsidiary to the class question --- a theoretical position that had practical consequences.^[Hal Draper, *Karl Marx's Theory of Revolution*, vol. 1 (New York: Monthly Review Press, 1977), 310--45.]

The German Social Democratic Party (SPD) under August Bebel and Clara Zetkin developed the most systematic Marxist approach to the "woman question" in the pre-revolutionary period. Bebel's *Woman and Socialism* (1879) argued that women's oppression was rooted in the development of private property and would be resolved through socialist revolution.^[August Bebel, *Woman and Socialism* (New York: Socialist Literature Company, 1910), 15--45.] Zetkin, while leading the women's secretariat of the SPD, insisted that working-class women's emancipation required alliance with the proletarian movement, but she also fought within the party for specific attention to women's issues.^[Clara Zetkin, "The Question of Women Workers and Women at the Present Time," in *Clara Zetkin: Selected Writings*, ed. Philip S. Foner (New York: International Publishers, 1984), 45--57.]

The early Soviet experiment was instructive not only for its achievements but for its swift abandonment. The Zhenotdel (Women's Department) of the Communist Party, established in 1919 under the leadership of Alexandra Kollontai and Inessa Armand, pursued an ambitious program of women's liberation: establishing childcare facilities, promoting women's education, combating domestic violence, and attempting to socialize housework.^[Alexandra Kollontai, *Selected Writings* (New York: W.W. Norton, 1977), 150--80.] Kollontai's theoretical work went further than any classical Marxist text in arguing that the liberation of women required not merely economic equality but the transformation of intimate life --- including sexuality, marriage, and the family.^[Alexandra Kollontai, *The Social Basis of the Woman Question* (Moscow: Communist Party of the Soviet Union, 1919), 25--60.]

The dissolution of the Zhenotdel in 1930 marked the end of this experiment. The Stalinist leadership, fearing the social disruption that genuine women's liberation entailed and needing to stabilize the family for industrial reproduction, dismantled the institutional infrastructure of feminist organizing within the party. The lesson was not that the Marxist method was incapable of addressing gender oppression but that the political will to do so could be defeated by bureaucratic conservatism.^[Elizabeth A. Wood, *The Baba and the Comrade: Gender and Politics in Revolutionary Russia* (Bloomington: Indiana University Press, 1997), 200--230.]

The tradition of socialist feminism from Alexandra Kollontai to the present demonstrates that Marxism contains within itself the theoretical resources for a full analysis of gender and sexual oppression. The limitation has not been theoretical but political. The Unified Blueprint draws on this suppressed tradition, reconnecting Marxism with its most radical implications for social liberation.

## 1.2 The Dialectics of Social Hierarchy

Dialectical materialism teaches that social development proceeds through the resolution of internal contradictions. ^[V.I. Lenin, "On the Question of Dialectics," in *Lenin Collected Works*, vol. 38 (Moscow: Progress Publishers, 1976), 355--57.] A contradiction, in the dialectical sense, is not a logical inconsistency but an objective tension within a system that drives its development. Capitalism contains the contradiction between socialized production and private appropriation; the revolutionary resolution of this contradiction requires the overthrow of capitalist relations.

The central philosophical claim of this book is that racism, heterosexism, cissexism, and other forms of social hierarchy constitute internal contradictions *within the working class itself* that must be resolved for proletarian unity to be achieved. These are not external impositions brought into the working class from the outside; they are structural features of capitalist social relations that fragment and divide the very agent of revolutionary transformation.

To see this, we must move beyond the notion of ideology as mere false consciousness. Louis Althusser's concept of interpellation --- the process by which individuals are constituted as subjects through ideology --- helps explain how racial and gendered identities are produced and reproduced within capitalist social formations.^[Louis Althusser, "Ideology and Ideological State Apparatuses," in *Lenin and Philosophy and Other Essays* (New York: Monthly Review Press, 1971), 127--86.] But Althusser's structuralism risks replacing one determinism (economic) with another (ideological). A properly dialectical approach recognizes that racial and gender ideologies have material effects precisely because they are anchored in the material relations of production and reproduction.

Consider the dialectical relationship between race and class under capitalism. The super-exploitation of racialized workers --- paying them less for equivalent labor, excluding them from better-paid positions, subjecting them to greater violence and discipline --- does not merely harm those workers individually. It depresses wages for the entire working class, creates a reserve army of labor that capital can deploy against organized workers, and generates social divisions that prevent the formation of class consciousness.^[David Roediger, *The Wages of Whiteness: Race and the Making of the American Working Class* (London: Verso, 1991), 13--15.] White workers who accept racist ideologies in exchange for a "psychological wage" are not simply mistaken; they are participating in a real, though ultimately self-defeating, material transaction.^[W.E.B. Du Bois, *Black Reconstruction in America* (New York: Oxford University Press, 2007), 700--701.]

The dialectic of gender and sexuality operates similarly. Women's unpaid domestic labor reproduces the workforce at minimal cost to capital, while the ideological construction of gender roles assigns different positions in the labor market to men and women.^[Mariarosa Dalla Costa and Selma James, *The Power of Women and the Subversion of the Community* (Bristol: Falling Wall Press, 1972), 19--34.] The oppression of sexual and gender minorities serves a specific material function: it enforces the gender binary and heterosexual family form that organizes both reproductive labor and consumption.^[John D'Emilio, "Capitalism and Gay Identity," in *Powers of Desire: The Politics of Sexuality*, ed. Ann Snitow, Christine Stansell, and Sharon Thompson (New York: Monthly Review Press, 1983), 100--113.]

These contradictions must be resolved, not deferred. The negation of the negation --- the dialectical process by which a contradiction is superseded at a higher level --- applies as much to social hierarchy as to class exploitation. The working class must negate racism and heterosexism within itself to achieve the higher unity necessary for revolutionary transformation. A proletariat that remains divided by race and sexuality cannot constitute itself as a class for itself in the full Marxian sense.

## 1.3 Base and Superstructure Revisited

The base-superstructure model is perhaps the most misunderstood and most mechanically applied concept in the Marxist tradition. Marx's famous formulation in the 1859 Preface to *A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy* describes the economic structure as "the real foundation, on which arises a legal and political superstructure."^[Karl Marx, "Preface to *A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy*," in *Marx-Engels Collected Works*, vol. 29 (Moscow: Progress Publishers, 1987), 262--63.] Engels, toward the end of his life, made clear that this was never intended as a one-way determinism:

> According to the materialist conception of history, the ultimately determining element in history is the production and reproduction of real life. More than this neither Marx nor I ever asserted. Hence if somebody twists this into saying that the economic element is the only determining one, he transforms that proposition into a meaningless, abstract, senseless phrase.^[Friedrich Engels, "Letter to Joseph Bloch," September 21--22, 1890, in *Marx-Engels Collected Works*, vol. 49 (Moscow: Progress Publishers, 2001), 33--37.]

This clarification opens the door to a more sophisticated understanding of the relationship between economic structure and social hierarchy. The orthodox "wait until after the revolution" position --- the claim that racial and gender oppression will automatically dissolve once capitalism is abolished --- relies on a crude base-superstructure model in which the superstructure is merely an epiphenomenon of the economic base. If Engels is correct that the economic is only "ultimately" (and not exclusively) determining, then social hierarchies have a relative autonomy and require specific theoretical attention and practical intervention.

But the argument of this book goes further. We contend that racial and gender oppression are not merely superstructural; they are constitutive of the base itself. The "production and reproduction of real life" to which Engels refers is not a gender-neutral or race-neutral process. The reproduction of labor power occurs through the family, which is a gendered institution. The segmentation of labor markets occurs along racial lines, creating different "rates of exploitation" for different groups.^[Nancy Fraser, "Behind Marx's Hidden Abode: For an Expanded Conception of Capitalism," *New Left Review* 86 (2014): 55--72.]

The social reproduction theory developed by feminist Marxists since the 1970s provides a framework for understanding this. Lise Vogel argued that Marx's analysis of capitalism must be expanded to include the "domestic labor" that reproduces labor power as a commodity.^[Lise Vogel, *Marxism and the Oppression of Women: Toward a Unitary Theory* (New Brunswick: Rutgers University Press, 1983), 134--60.] More recent work has shown how racialized and gendered divisions in social reproduction --- who cooks, who cleans, who cares for children, who is available for paid employment --- are not external to capital accumulation but integral to it.^[Tithi Bhattacharya, ed., *Social Reproduction Theory: Remapping Class, Recentering Oppression* (London: Pluto Press, 2017), 1--20.]

Similarly, the racialized division of labor is not a superstructural residue of slavery and colonialism; it is a structural feature of capitalist accumulation that persists and adapts across different modes of production. Cedric Robinson's theory of racial capitalism demonstrated that capitalism emerged within and through racial hierarchies, not despite them.^[Cedric Robinson, *Black Marxism: The Making of the Black Radical Tradition* (Chapel Hill: University of North Carolina Press, 1983), 1--17.] The implication for socialist construction is clear: if racial and gender oppression are constitutive of capitalism, then their abolition cannot be left to the automatic workings of post-revolutionary economic development. They require specific, conscious, and legally-enforced intervention.

## 1.4 Refuting the Objections: Class Reductionism and Its Defenses

The most common objection to the project of this book is what we may call the "primacy of class" thesis. This argument holds that while racial, gender, and sexual oppression are real and deplorable, they are ultimately effects of class society and will be resolved through the abolition of class. To focus on them is to risk dividing the working class and diverting energy from the primary struggle.

This objection has a long pedigree within Marxism, and it must be taken seriously. Its most sophisticated formulation appears in the work of Ellen Meiksins Wood, who argued that the "retreat from class" in contemporary left theory represented an abandonment of Marxism's unique contribution to social analysis.^[Ellen Meiksins Wood, *The Retreat from Class: A New 'True' Socialism* (London: Verso, 1986), 1--15.] More recently, critics of "intersectionality" from a Marxist perspective have charged that the concept fragments working-class politics and replaces material analysis with identity politics.^[Walter Benn Michaels, *The Trouble with Diversity: How We Learned to Love Identity and Ignore Inequality* (New York: Metropolitan Books, 2006), 1--25.]

These critiques contain genuine insights. The liberal form of intersectionality, detached from any critique of capitalism, can indeed become a mechanism for managing diversity within capitalist social relations rather than challenging them. Kimberlé Crenshaw's original formulation of intersectionality was rooted in Black feminist legal activism against multiple forms of marginalization.^[Kimberlé Crenshaw, "Demarginalizing the Intersection of Race and Sex: A Black Feminist Critique of Antidiscrimination Doctrine," *University of Chicago Legal Forum* 1989, no. 1 (1989): 139--67.] But the concept has been appropriated by liberal and neoliberal frameworks that treat recognition without redistribution as sufficient.^[Nancy Fraser, "From Redistribution to Recognition? Dilemmas of Justice in a 'Post-Socialist' Age," *New Left Review* 212 (1995): 68--93.]

The response to the class primacy objection is not to abandon class analysis but to deepen it. The recognition that racism and heterosexism divide the working class is not a departure from Marxism but a return to its most fundamental premises. Marx did not argue that the working class is spontaneously united; he argued that it must *become* united through struggle. The overcoming of internal divisions is a necessary moment in the formation of class consciousness, not a distraction from it.

Moreover, the class primacy thesis rests on an empirical claim that has been falsified by history. No socialist state has ever automatically eliminated racial or gender oppression through the simple fact of nationalizing the means of production. The Soviet Union, for all its achievements in women's education and employment, retained patriarchal structures that were only partially addressed.^[Wendy Z. Goldman, *Women at the Gates: Gender and Industry in Stalin's Russia* (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2002), 1--18.] The Chinese Revolution ended formal landlord exploitation but did not eliminate the subordination of women or ethnic minorities.^[Mobo Gao, *The Battle for China's Past: Mao and the Cultural Revolution* (London: Pluto Press, 2008), 75--90.] These failures were not accidents; they were consequences of a theoretical framework that treated social oppression as secondary and assumed its automatic disappearance.

The Leninist principle of "concrete analysis of concrete conditions" demands that we treat each form of oppression with specificity.^[Vladimir Lenin, "Once Again on the Trade Unions," in *Lenin Collected Works*, vol. 32 (Moscow: Progress Publishers, 1973), 93.] Racial oppression under capitalism has specific historical origins (colonialism, slavery) and specific mechanisms (segregated labor markets, mass incarceration) that require specific remedies. Gender oppression has specific structures (the patriarchal family, the gender division of labor) that are not reducible to class. Heteronormativity has specific institutions (the nuclear family, the gender binary) that predate capitalism and persist across modes of production, albeit in transformed forms.^[Judith Butler, *Gender Trouble: Feminism and the Subversion of Identity* (New York: Routledge, 1990), 1--35.]

A Marxism that cannot account for these specificities is not a scientific Marxism; it is a dogmatic Marxism that mistakes its abstractions for concrete reality.

### The Case of Sexuality: Beyond the Orthodox Framework

The Marxist tradition has been particularly weak in its theoretical treatment of sexuality. This weakness is not accidental but structural: the heteronormative family form is so deeply embedded in capitalist social reproduction that even revolutionary critics have had difficulty imagining its abolition. The result has been a persistent gap between Marxist theory and LGBTQ+ liberation movements that has weakened both.

The Frankfurt School's analysis of sexual repression under capitalism offered one avenue for integration. Herbert Marcuse's *Eros and Civilization* argued that capitalism requires the sublimation of sexual energies into productive labor, and that sexual liberation is therefore a necessary component of human emancipation.^[Herbert Marcuse, *Eros and Civilization: A Philosophical Inquiry into Freud* (Boston: Beacon Press, 1955), 80--110.] Wilhelm Reich's *The Mass Psychology of Fascism* linked sexual repression to authoritarian personality formation and argued that sexual liberation was a prerequisite for revolutionary consciousness.^[Wilhelm Reich, *The Mass Psychology of Fascism* (New York: Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 1970), 30--60.]

However, the Frankfurt School's analysis, for all its insights, remained within a psychological framework that did not fully integrate sexuality into the materialist analysis of production and reproduction. It was left to later queer Marxists to develop a more rigorous synthesis. John D'Emilio's groundbreaking essay "Capitalism and Gay Identity" argued that capitalism itself created the conditions for gay identity formation by freeing individuals from the constraints of the family-based household economy.^[John D'Emilio, "Capitalism and Gay Identity," 100--113.] This insight --- that capitalism simultaneously produces and represses homosexual identity --- reveals the dialectical character of sexuality under capitalist social relations.

The practical implication is that socialist construction must include a positive program for sexual liberation, not merely the decriminalization of homosexuality. This means recognizing same-sex relationships, supporting diverse family forms, providing gender-affirming healthcare, and combating heteronormative ideology through education and culture. A socialism that fails to do this is not only incomplete; it is reproducing one of capitalism's most effective mechanisms of social control.

## 1.5 Toward Intersectional Materialism

What emerges from this analysis is a theoretical synthesis we term *Intersectional Materialism*. This framework is not an eclectic combination of Marxism and identity politics. It is a rigorous development of the dialectical and materialist method applied to the concrete totality of social oppression under capitalism and beyond.

Intersectional Materialism rests on the following theoretical propositions:

1. **Totality**: Class, race, gender, sexuality, and other social relations constitute a single, internally differentiated totality. None can be understood in isolation from the others; none can be reduced to the others.

2. **Contradiction**: Social hierarchies are internal contradictions within the working class that must be resolved for revolutionary unity to be achieved. The resolution of these contradictions is a material precondition for socialist construction.

3. **Determination**: The economic is "determinant in the last instance" (Althusser), but the "last instance" never arrives in pure form. Social hierarchies have relative autonomy and specific effects that must be analyzed with precision.

4. **Transformation**: The abolition of social hierarchies requires both structural economic transformation (the abolition of capitalism) and specific, conscious intervention in the superstructure (law, culture, ideology). Neither is sufficient alone.

5. **Unity**: Social liberation and class liberation are not competing goals but two moments of a single revolutionary process. The slogan "No socialism without liberation; no liberation without socialism" is not a rhetorical flourish but a theoretical necessity.

This framework avoids both the Scylla of class reductionism (which cannot account for the persistence of social oppression after economic transformation) and the Charybdis of liberal identity politics (which treats recognition as a substitute for redistribution). It is a unified theory for a unified movement.

## 1.6 The Philosophical Grounding of Change

The epigraph to this book could well be Heraclitus's observation, quoted with approval by both Marx and Lenin, that "everything flows and nothing stays."^[Vladimir Lenin, "On the Question of Dialectics," 357.] The dialectical principle that nothing is permanent except change itself provides the philosophical foundation for the evolution of socialist law and social relations. If social hierarchies were natural and eternal, they could not be abolished. But the dialectic teaches us that they are historical products, arising under specific material conditions and susceptible to transformation when those conditions change.

The conservative within the socialist movement --- the figure who says "first the revolution, then we will address social questions" --- is in fact betraying the dialectic. He or she treats the present configuration of social relations as a fixed reality that must be worked around rather than a dynamic contradiction that must be resolved. The truly revolutionary position is that social transformation must begin the moment state power is achieved, through the conscious application of legal and political force to uproot the material and ideological foundations of oppression.

This is not voluntarism; it is the application of the materialist conception of history to its own revolutionary practice. The superstructure, Engels reminds us, "reacts upon the economic base."^[Engels, "Letter to Joseph Bloch," 36.] Law, culture, and ideology are not passive reflections of economic relations; they are active forces that can accelerate or retard historical development. A socialist state that fails to use these forces to abolish social hierarchy is not being "patient" or "pragmatic"; it is failing to fulfill its revolutionary mandate.

The philosophical shift proposed in this chapter --- from class-reductionism to intersectional materialism --- is therefore not a revision of Marxism but its completion. It takes the dialectical and materialist method seriously and applies it without reservation, following the analysis wherever it leads. The chapters that follow will trace the implications of this shift for economics, law, political strategy, and daily life under socialism.
