# Module 12 — Sleep-Wake Regulation Phenotypes

This module reclassifies sleep-wake disorders as dysregulation of the neurobiological circuits governing arousal, sleep drive, circadian timing, and sleep-state transitions. Allostatic load accumulates through persistent hyperarousal, excessive sleep drive, ventilatory instability, circadian misalignment, or intrusion of REM/NREM mechanisms into inappropriate states.

### 12.1 Sleep-Onset Maintenance Inertia Phenotype (formerly Insomnia Disorder 307.42 / F51.01)

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Persistent hyperarousal of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis with failure of the ventrolateral preoptic nucleus (VLPO) to inhibit ascending arousal systems (orexinergic, histaminergic, noradrenergic). Conditioned cortical arousal overrides homeostatic sleep drive, creating a feedforward loop of sleep effort, frustration, and sustained sympathetic activation.

**Somatic Architecture Table**

| DSM-5 Criterion | Allostatic Interpretation | Somatic Origin |
|---|---|---|
| A1: Difficulty initiating sleep | Failure of VLPO GABAergic inhibition of tuberomammillary nucleus | Sustained histaminergic tone; elevated cortisol at sleep onset |
| A2: Difficulty maintaining sleep | Nocturnal HPA reactivation; inability to sustain NREM sleep maintenance | Elevated ACTH/cortisol pulses in latter half of sleep period |
| A3: Early-morning awakening | Phase-advanced circadian drive coupled with hypercortisolemia | Cortisol awakening response onset 1–3 hours premature |
| B: Distress/impairment | Cortical energy depletion from sustained vigilance | Prefrontal metabolic dysregulation; reduced EEG delta power |
| C: ≥3 nights/week | Recurrent entrainment of conditioned arousal | Repeated HPA sensitization |
| D: ≥3 months | Consolidation of maladaptive sleep architecture | Reduced hippocampal volume; altered GABA-A receptor density |
| E: Adequate opportunity | Homeostatic sleep drive present but overridden by arousal | Elevated norepinephrine; normal adenosine accumulation |
| F–H: Exclusion criteria | Primary vs. secondary allostatic load differentiation | Differential etiology in brainstem, hypothalamic, or cortical circuits |

**Forensic Proxy Markers**

| Proxy | Palpation/Measurement Protocol | Pathological Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Resting heart rate at bedtime | Radial pulse palpation in seated position, 2 min before lights-out | >80 bpm sustained |
| Pupillary unrest in darkness | Observe pupillary diameter under dim light for 60 s | Fluctuation >0.5 mm |
| Sleep-onset latency (actigraphy) | 7-day wrist actigraphy: time from lights-off to first sleep epoch | Mean >30 min |
| Cognitive arousal index | Patient-reported pre-sleep racing thoughts (0–10 scale) | >4/10 consistent |

**Entropy Threshold**

| State | Criteria | Biological Markers |
|---|---|---|
| Protected Phenotype | Sleep latency <20 min, <2 awakenings/night, HR decline at sleep onset | Normal nocturnal BP dip; HRV high-frequency power intact |
| Transition Zone | Sleep latency 20–30 min, 2–3 awakenings, mild daytime fatigue | Evening cortisol 50–75th percentile; reduced delta power |
| Somatic Debt | Sleep latency >30 min, >3 awakenings, daytime impairment | HR >80 bpm bedtime; elevated nocturnal norepinephrine; lack of HRV circadian modulation |

**Biological Bill of Rights**

1. The right to verify arousal threshold by pupillary unrest index before labeling the sleep attempt as failed.
2. The right to distinguish conditioned arousal (salvageable) from structural VLPO deficiency (requires neuroprotection).
3. The right to pharmacological intervention only after 4 weeks of quantified actigraphy demonstrating threshold persistence.
4. The right to refuse sleep effort (forced sleep attempts worsen the allostatic load) and instead rest in dim light without performance demand.

### 12.2 Excessive Sleep Drive Phenotype (formerly Hypersomnolence Disorder 307.44 / F51.11)

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Heightened homeostatic sleep pressure with reduced threshold for sleep initiation despite adequate nocturnal sleep duration. Central nervous system hyporesponsiveness to wake-promoting signals from the reticular activating system and orexinergic projections, resulting in prolonged sleep episodes, sleep inertia, and involuntary microsleeps during low-stimulation states.

**Somatic Architecture Table**

| DSM-5 Criterion | Allostatic Interpretation | Somatic Origin |
|---|---|---|
| A1: Recurrent sleep lapses | Reduced orexinergic tone; impaired arousal maintenance | Reticular activating system hypoactivation |
| A2: Prolonged sleep >9 hr unrefreshing | Excess adenosine accumulation; reduced sleep efficiency per unit time | Impaired glymphatic clearance during sleep |
| A3: Difficulty awakening / sleep inertia | Delayed reactivation of ascending reticular system | Reduced locus coeruleus noradrenergic surge at sleep-wake transition |
| B: ≥3x/week, ≥3 months | Chronic failure of arousal threshold regulation | Downregulated histaminergic receptors |
| C: Distress/impairment | Sustained low-alertness state impairs prefrontal function | Reduced cerebral glucose metabolism in waking state |
| D–F: Exclusion criteria | Differentiation from narcolepsy, apnea, circadian, and substance effects | Structural vs. functional etiology in brainstem/thalamic circuits |

**Forensic Proxy Markers**

| Proxy | Palpation/Measurement Protocol | Pathological Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Sleep latency on MSLT | Mean time to sleep across 4–5 nap opportunities | <8 minutes |
| Sleep inertia duration (self-report, 7-day diary) | Time from awakening to subjective full alertness | >30 min consistently |
| Pupillary fatigue waves | Observe pupil diameter in dim light for 3 min without stimulation | Spontaneous miosis >1 mm within 3 min |
| Mean nocturnal sleep (actigraphy) | 7-day wrist actigraphy | >9 hr/24h with <85% efficiency |

**Entropy Threshold**

| State | Criteria | Biological Markers |
|---|---|---|
| Protected Phenotype | Sleep need 7–9 hr, awakening within 5 min, MSLT >10 min | Normal orexin levels; intact cortical EEG desynchronization on awakening |
| Transition Zone | Sleep need 9–10 hr, sleep inertia 15–30 min, occasional microsleeps | MSLT 8–10 min; borderline pupillary fatigue |
| Somatic Debt | Sleep need >10 hr, sleep inertia >30 min daily, involuntary naps | MSLT <8 min; spontaneous pupillary miosis; objective sleep efficiency <85% despite prolonged time in bed |

**Biological Bill of Rights**

1. The right to an objective sleep latency test before being labeled as having excessive sleep propensity, distinguishing low arousal from behavioral sleep restriction.
2. The right to differentiate sleep inertia from depressive anergia via actigraphy and pupillometry.
3. The right to treatment (wake-promoting agents, light therapy, scheduled napping) based on objective MSLT findings rather than subjective fatigue alone.
4. The right to exclude occult sleep apnea, iron deficiency, and circadian misalignment before accepting a diagnosis of primary hypersomnolence.

### 12.3 Orexinergic Collapse Phenotype (formerly Narcolepsy 347.00–347.10 / G47.419–G47.429)

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Autoimmune-mediated destruction of hypothalamic hypocretin (orexin)-producing neurons, resulting in failure to stabilize wake-sleep boundaries. Loss of orexinergic tone permits inappropriate intrusion of REM sleep atonia mechanisms into wakefulness (cataplexy) and destabilization of sleep-wake transitions, causing direct entry into REM sleep (SOREMPs) and fragmented nocturnal architecture.

**Somatic Architecture Table**

| DSM-5 Criterion | Allostatic Interpretation | Somatic Origin |
|---|---|---|
| A: Recurrent irrepressible need to sleep | Loss of orexin stabilization of arousal; inability to sustain wakefulness | Hypothalamic orexin neuron destruction; impaired lateral hypothalamic signaling to monoaminergic arousal centers |
| B1: Cataplexy | Inappropriate activation of REM-atonia GABA/glycinergic pathways during wakefulness triggered by positive affect | Pontine REM-on nuclei activation without cortical desynchronization; absent muscle tone |
| B2: Hypocretin deficiency ≤1/3 normal | Autoimmune destruction of orexin neurons; HLA-DQB1*06:02 association | CSF hypocretin-1 ≤110 pg/mL; loss of hypothalamic neuropeptide reserve |
| B3: SOREMPs (REM ≤15 min) | Collapse of NREM-REM sequencing; direct intrusion of REM from wake | Absent orexinergic suppression of REM-on neurons during sleep onset |

**Forensic Proxy Markers**

| Proxy | Palpation/Measurement Protocol | Pathological Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Cataplexy trigger test | Ask patient re: laughter/joke-induced jaw drop, head bob, or knee buckle | Any positive event in past month |
| MSLT mean sleep latency | 4–5 nap opportunities at 2-hr intervals | ≤8 minutes |
| SOREMP count (MSLT) | Number of naps with REM onset within 15 min | ≥2 of 4–5 naps |
| CSF hypocretin-1 | Lumbar puncture; ELISA assay | ≤110 pg/mL or <1/3 control mean |

**Entropy Threshold**

| State | Criteria | Biological Markers |
|---|---|---|
| Protected Phenotype | No cataplexy, stable sleep schedule, MSLT >10 min | Normal CSF hypocretin; HLA-negative |
| Transition Zone | Occasional sleepiness, rare cataplexy (<1/week), 1 SOREMP | Low-normal hypocretin; one SOREMP on MSLT |
| Somatic Debt | Daily cataplexy, multiple SOREMPs, fragmented sleep, MSLT <5 min | CSF hypocretin ≤110 pg/mL; ≥2 SOREMPs; HLA-DQB1*06:02 positive |

**Biological Bill of Rights**

1. The right to CSF hypocretin-1 quantification as the gold-standard confirmation, not solely MSLT-based inference.
2. The right to differentiate cataplexy from pseudocataplexy via reflex testing during an episode (areflexia = genuine).
3. The right to treatment with sodium oxybate or pitolisant based on objective biomarker evidence, not merely subjective sleepiness.
4. The right to exclude autoimmune triggers (streptococcal, H1N1, vaccination history) before accepting the phenotype as idiopathic.

### 12.4 Pharyngeal Collapse Apnea Phenotype (formerly Obstructive Sleep Apnea Hypopnea 327.23 / G47.33)

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Structural or functional narrowing of the pharyngeal airway resulting in repeated collapse during sleep when negative inspiratory pressure exceeds dilator muscle compensation. Each collapse causes transient hypoxemia, hypercapnia, and cortical arousal, fragmenting sleep architecture and activating the sympathetic nervous system, leading to cumulative cardiovascular allostatic load.

**Somatic Architecture Table**

| DSM-5 Criterion | Allostatic Interpretation | Somatic Origin |
|---|---|---|
| A1: ≥5 apneas/hypopneas/hour + symptoms | Pharyngeal dilator failure during sleep; insufficient genioglossus compensatory activation to maintain airway patency | Anatomic narrowing (obesity, retrognathia, tonsillar hypertrophy); neuromuscular coordination failure |
| A2: ≥15 events/hour regardless of symptoms | Severe collapse threshold; cumulative hypoxemic burden | Desaturation nadirs <90%; sustained sympathetic activation |
| Nocturnal disturbances (snoring, gasping) | Turbulent airflow through narrowed oropharynx; arousal-terminated apnea | Palatal and pharyngeal wall vibration; transient asphyxia |
| Daytime sleepiness/fatigue symptoms | Sleep fragmentation from repeated arousals disrupts restorative slow-wave sleep | Reduced delta power; increased stage N1 sleep; elevated norepinephrine |

**Forensic Proxy Markers**

| Proxy | Palpation/Measurement Protocol | Pathological Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Neck circumference | Tape measure at level of cricothyroid membrane | >40 cm (M) / >37 cm (F) |
| STOP-BANG score | 8-item questionnaire (snoring, tiredness, observed apnea, pressure, BMI, age, neck, gender) | ≥3 positive items |
| Waist-to-height ratio | Waist circumference / height | >0.5 |
| Nocturnal pulse oximetry | Home overnight oximeter; 3% oxygen desaturation index (ODI) | ODI ≥5 events/hour |

**Entropy Threshold**

| State | Criteria | Biological Markers |
|---|---|---|
| Protected Phenotype | AHI <5, no desaturation, no daytime impairment | Normal nocturnal BP dip; SpO2 >95% during sleep |
| Transition Zone | AHI 5–15, mild symptoms, ODI 5–14 | Intermittent desaturation 90–94%; mild sleep fragmentation |
| Somatic Debt | AHI ≥15 or ≥5 with symptoms, ODI ≥15 | SpO2 <90% for >10% of sleep; nocturnal BP non-dipper pattern; elevated morning catecholamines |

**Biological Bill of Rights**

1. The right to home sleep testing rather than in-laboratory PSG as the initial diagnostic tier when pretest probability is high.
2. The right to differentiate positional from supine-independent apnea before committing to lifelong positive airway pressure therapy.
3. The right to assess craniofacial anatomy (Mallampati score, retrognathia) as a modifiable structural contributor before accepting airway therapy as the sole solution.
4. The right to cardiovascular risk quantification (nocturnal BP monitoring, morning catecholamines) as part of the severity assessment.

### 12.5 Ventilatory Control Instability Phenotype (formerly Central Sleep Apnea 327.21, 786.04, 780.57 / G47.31, R06.3, G47.37)

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Dysregulation of brainstem ventilatory control centers resulting in high-loop-gain instability: the chemoreflex response to CO2 fluctuation is excessive, causing ventilatory overshoot, hypocapnia, and subsequent central apnea when CO2 falls below the apneic threshold. In opioid-related subtypes, respiratory rhythm generators in the medulla are directly suppressed, producing ataxic breathing and central events.

**Somatic Architecture Table**

| DSM-5 Criterion | Allostatic Interpretation | Somatic Origin |
|---|---|---|
| A: ≥5 central apneas/hour on PSG | Instability in brainstem CO2 chemosensitivity; high loop gain | Medullary chemoreceptor hypersensitivity; exaggerated ventilatory response to PaCO2 fluctuation |
| Idiopathic subtype | Primary chemoreflex instability without structural cause | Elevated controller gain; low-normal awake PaCO2 |
| Cheyne-Stokes breathing | Delayed circulation time + heightened chemosensitivity creates periodic breathing | Heart failure → prolonged circulation → feedback delay → oscillation |
| Opioid-related subtype | Opioid suppression of medullary pre-Botzinger complex rhythmogenesis | Reduced hypoxic ventilatory drive; ataxic breathing pattern |

**Forensic Proxy Markers**

| Proxy | Palpation/Measurement Protocol | Pathological Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Awake PaCO2 | Arterial blood gas | ≤38 mmHg (idiopathic); ≥45 mmHg (opioid-related) |
| Ejection fraction (Cheyne-Stokes) | Echocardiogram | LVEF <45% |
| Opioid dose screening | Chart review: daily morphine milligram equivalents | >50 MME/day |
| Central apnea index (PSG) | Apneas without respiratory effort on polysomnography | ≥5/hr; >50% of total respiratory events |

**Entropy Threshold**

| State | Criteria | Biological Markers |
|---|---|---|
| Protected Phenotype | Central apnea index <5/hr, no Cheyne-Stokes, no SDB symptoms | Normal awake PaCO2; LVEF >50% |
| Transition Zone | CAI 5–14/hr, mild sleep fragmentation | Mildly elevated loop gain on PSG; borderline CO2 response |
| Somatic Debt | CAI ≥15/hr, Cheyne-Stokes with heart failure, or opioid-related with ataxic breathing | LVEF <45%; PaCO2 <35 or >45; OSA-central overlap >50% central events |

**Biological Bill of Rights**

1. The right to differentiate central from obstructive events by esophageal manometry or diaphragm EMG, not by thoracic effort bands alone.
2. The right to assess loop gain quantitatively (CPAP withdrawal protocol) before labeling the apnea as idiopathic or treatment-emergent.
3. The right to optimization of heart failure therapy before treating Cheyne-Stokes breathing with positive airway pressure.
4. The right to opioid dose reduction evaluation when central apnea is detected in the context of chronic opioid therapy, rather than accepting ventilation support as the first-line adaptation.

### 12.6 Hypoventilatory CO2 Retention Phenotype (formerly Sleep-Related Hypoventilation 327.24–327.26 / G47.34–G47.36)

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Failure of the respiratory system to maintain adequate alveolar ventilation during sleep, resulting in elevated PaCO2 and sustained hypoxemia. The mechanism divides into "won't breathe" (blunted central chemosensitivity to CO2) versus "can't breathe" (neuromuscular pump failure, chest wall restriction, or increased load from obesity). Unlike apnea phenotypes, the respiratory pattern shows sustained hypoventilation rather than discrete events.

**Somatic Architecture Table**

| DSM-5 Criterion | Allostatic Interpretation | Somatic Origin |
|---|---|---|
| A: PSG evidence of elevated CO2 with decreased respiration | Blunted chemoresponsiveness (central) or pump failure (peripheral); failure to augment minute ventilation during sleep | Medullary chemoreceptor insensitivity (idiopathic/congenital); diaphragm weakness (neuromuscular); increased work of breathing (obesity, COPD) |
| Idiopathic subtype | Congenital or acquired central chemoreceptor hyposensitivity | PHOX2B mutation (congenital central alveolar hypoventilation); reduced ventilatory response to hypercapnia |
| Comorbid subtype (obesity, NM, pulmonary) | Mechanical load exceeds respiratory pump capacity; ventilation-perfusion mismatch | Reduced chest wall compliance; diaphragm flattening; airway obstruction |
| B: Not better explained by another sleep disorder | Distinction from OSA/CSA: sustained vs. phasic hypoventilation | Prolonged desaturation >5 min without discrete apnea events |

**Forensic Proxy Markers**

| Proxy | Palpation/Measurement Protocol | Pathological Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Awake PaCO2 | Arterial blood gas | >45 mmHg |
| Nocturnal SpO2 pattern | Home oximetry: sustained desaturation <90% without cyclic pattern | >5 min at <90% OR >30% of sleep time <90% |
| BMI | Weight / height² | >30 kg/m² (obesity hypoventilation) |
| Forced vital capacity (FVC) | Spirometry, supine vs. upright | >20% drop supine suggests diaphragm weakness |

**Entropy Threshold**

| State | Criteria | Biological Markers |
|---|---|---|
| Protected Phenotype | Awake PaCO2 <45, nocturnal SpO2 >90%, no daytime hypercapnia | Normal ventilatory response to CO2; intact diaphragm function |
| Transition Zone | Awake PaCO2 45–49, intermittent nocturnal desaturation 85–89% | Borderline FVC; mild obesity (BMI 30–35) |
| Somatic Debt | Awake PaCO2 ≥50, sustained nocturnal SpO2 <85%, cor pulmonale signs | PHOX2B mutation; FVC <50% predicted; right heart strain on echo |

**Biological Bill of Rights**

1. The right to transcutaneous or end-tidal CO2 monitoring during PSG rather than oximetry alone for hypoventilation diagnosis.
2. The right to differentiate "won't breathe" (central chemoresponsiveness testing) from "can't breathe" (PFTs, diaphragm ultrasound) to guide treatment.
3. The right to obesity hypoventilation evaluation (awake PaCO2, bicarbonate) before attributing sleep-disordered breathing to OSA alone.
4. The right to genetic testing (PHOX2B) in early-onset or familial cases of central hypoventilation before labeling as idiopathic.

### 12.7 Circadian Phase Misalignment Phenotypes (formerly Circadian Rhythm Sleep-Wake Disorders 307.45 / G47.21–G47.26)

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Persistent or recurrent mismatch between endogenous circadian pacemaker timing (suprachiasmatic nucleus) and the environmental/social sleep-wake schedule. The SCN fails to synchronize peripheral clocks to external light-dark cycles due to behavioral, genetic, or environmental factors (reduced light exposure, altered period length, temporal isolation), resulting in misaligned melatonin secretion, core body temperature nadir, and cortisol acrophase.

**Somatic Architecture Table**

| DSM-5 Criterion | Allostatic Interpretation | Somatic Origin |
|---|---|---|
| A: Sleep disruption due to circadian misalignment | Phase mismatch between SCN output and behavioral sleep scheduling | Delayed/advanced melatonin onset; shifted CBT nadir; altered PER gene expression |
| B: Insomnia and/or sleepiness | Circadian drive for wake conflicts with scheduled sleep, or vice versa | Inappropriate timing of melatonin secretion relative to desired sleep window |
| C: Distress/impairment | Cumulative sleep loss from forced desynchrony | Elevated evening cortisol (delayed phase); premature cortisol rise (advanced phase) |
| Delayed phase | Intrinsic period >24.2 hr and/or hypersensitivity to evening light + hyposensitivity to morning light | Delayed melatonin onset >2 hr relative to desired bedtime; PER3, CKIε polymorphisms |
| Advanced phase | Intrinsic period <24.0 hr; familial PER2/CKIδ mutations | Advanced melatonin onset; early CBT nadir; autosomal dominant inheritance |
| Irregular type | Absent circadian organization; fragmented sleep-wake across 24 hr | Low-amplitude or absent melatonin rhythm; common in neurodegeneration |
| Non-24-hour type | Free-running rhythm not entrained to 24 hr; daily drift of sleep-wake | Absent light entrainment (blind); circadian period >24.2 hr |
| Shift work type | Forced desynchrony between work schedule and endogenous circadian timing | Chronic circadian disruption; social jet lag |

**Forensic Proxy Markers**

| Proxy | Palpation/Measurement Protocol | Pathological Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Dim light melatonin onset (DLMO) | Salivary melatonin every 30 min in dim light <30 lux beginning 5–6 hr before habitual bedtime | Phase >2 hr from desired sleep onset |
| Actigraphy (7–14 day) | Wrist actigraphy including free days; calculate midline estimating statistic of rhythm (MESOR) | Phase angle between sleep midpoint and desired midpoint >2 hr |
| Morningness-Eveningness Questionnaire (MEQ) | 19-item self-report | <41 (evening type); >59 (morning type) |
| Core body temperature nadir | Continuous temperature monitoring (ingestible sensor or skin patch) | Nadir shifted relative to sleep midpoint >2 hr |

**Entropy Threshold**

| State | Criteria | Biological Markers |
|---|---|---|
| Protected Phenotype | Sleep-wake aligned with social schedule, phase angle <1 hr, no daytime impairment | DLMO aligned with desired sleep onset; normal actigraphy MESOR |
| Transition Zone | Phase angle 1–2 hr, mild sleep-onset difficulty or early awakening, compensated by napping | DLMO delayed/advanced 1–2 hr; partial social jet lag |
| Somatic Debt | Phase angle >2 hr, chronic insomnia/sleepiness, occupational/social failure | DLMO >2 hr misaligned; actigraphy shows consistent free-run or fragmentation; CBT nadir during desired waking hours |

**Biological Bill of Rights**

1. The right to DLMO measurement (salivary melatonin) as the confirmatory biomarker before circadian phenotyping.
2. The right to distinguish circadian misalignment from insomnia disorder via 7-day actigraphy including free days.
3. The right to timed light therapy and melatonin administration based on phase-response curves rather than arbitrary dosing.
4. The right to exclude comorbid depression and shift work before labeling the circadian disruption as a primary entrainment failure.

### 12.8 NREM Dissociative Arousal Phenotype (formerly Non-Rapid Eye Movement Sleep Arousal Disorders: Sleepwalking Type / Sleep Terror Type 307.46 / F51.3–F51.4)

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Incomplete dissociation between NREM sleep (slow-wave) and wakefulness: brain regions involved in complex motor behavior activate while prefrontal cortices remain in sleep. The thalamocortical gate fails to open fully, preventing conscious awareness and memory encoding, while motor pattern generators execute species-typical fight-flight or ambulatory programs. Autonomic activation is maximal in sleep terrors, minimal in quiet sleepwalking.

**Somatic Architecture Table**

| DSM-5 Criterion | Allostatic Interpretation | Somatic Origin |
|---|---|---|
| A1: Sleepwalking episodes | Motor pattern generators disinhibited from NREM; prefrontal offline | Brainstem locomotor center activation without cortical integration |
| A2: Sleep terrors with autonomic surge | Sympathetic flood during incomplete arousal from slow-wave sleep; amygdala activation without hippocampal contextualization | Tachycardia (2–3x baseline), mydriasis, tachypnea from brainstem autonomic nuclei |
| B: No dream recall | Absent cortical binding of sensory experience into narrative | Hippocampal-PFC connectivity suppressed; occipital cortex offline |
| C: Amnesia for episodes | Failure of hippocampal memory consolidation during incomplete arousal | Hippocampal theta absent during episode |

**Forensic Proxy Markers**

| Proxy | Palpation/Measurement Protocol | Pathological Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Episode timing relative to sleep onset | Caregiver log of event time from lights-out | First third of sleep period (within 2–3 hr) |
| Confusional arousal on awakening | Attempt to awaken patient 30 min after sleep onset; observe response | Failure to achieve full orientation within 5 min |
| Sleep deprivation history | 7-day actigraphy or sleep diary | <6 hr average sleep or >2 hr variability |
| PSG with video, sleep-deprived | Overnight PSG following 24-hr sleep deprivation to increase slow-wave pressure | Episode captured from N3 sleep with theta/delta EEG during behavior |

**Entropy Threshold**

| State | Criteria | Biological Markers |
|---|---|---|
| Protected Phenotype | No arousals, full awakening from any stage, no episodes reported | Normal slow-wave fragmentation index |
| Transition Zone | Rare episodes (<1/month), triggered by sleep deprivation or fever | Increased N3 instability on PSG; provoked by sleep deprivation |
| Somatic Debt | Frequent episodes (>1/week), injury risk, forensic involvement (driving, sexual behavior) | Documented from N3 on PSG; comorbid sleep apnea or RLS as triggers |

**Biological Bill of Rights**

1. The right to video-PSG confirmation before forensic attribution of nocturnal behavior, especially in sexsomnia or violence cases.
2. The right to treat trigger factors (sleep apnea, sleep deprivation, alcohol, sedatives) before accepting the phenotype as a lifelong trait.
3. The right to distinguish dissociative fugue (wake-origin, trauma history) from NREM arousal (precipitous from sleep, amnesia).
4. The right to safety documentation (environmental precautions: locks, alarms, sharp-object removal) as first-line intervention.

### 12.9 Threat-Rehearsal REM Phenotype (formerly Nightmare Disorder 307.47 / F51.5)

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Dysregulation of fear-extinction circuitry during REM sleep: the amygdala generates threat simulations without adequate prefrontal-limbic modulation. Normally, REM sleep serves emotional memory consolidation and fear extinction via hippocampal-prefrontal dialogue; in this phenotype, the simulation becomes excessive, producing dysphoric narrative content with sufficient emotional salience to trigger awakening and sustained amygdala activation post-arousal.

**Somatic Architecture Table**

| DSM-5 Criterion | Allostatic Interpretation | Somatic Origin |
|---|---|---|
| A: Extended dysphoric dreams, usually in 2nd half of sleep | Elevated amygdala activation during REM; insufficient medial prefrontal downregulation of limbic fear responses | Heightened REM density; increased phasic REM events |
| B: Rapid orientation on awakening | Intact ascending reticular activation; immediate cortical arousal | Immediate EEG desynchronization; normal PFC function post-arousal |
| C: Distress/impairment | Elevated post-REM sympathetic state persists into waking | Increased heart rate and cortisol on awakening; subjective fear |
| D–E: Exclusion of substance/medical causes | Differentiate from REM rebound (antidepressant withdrawal), PTSD, narcolepsy | Polysomnography shows REM-sudden awakening temporal relationship |

**Forensic Proxy Markers**

| Proxy | Palpation/Measurement Protocol | Pathological Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Nightmare frequency log | Patient-recorded count of dream-recall awakenings with dysphoric content | ≥1 episode/week |
| REM sleep fragmentation index | Actigraphy or PSG: awakenings from REM periods in 2nd half of night | >2 REM awakenings/night |
| Traumatic history screen | PTSD Checklist (PCL-5) or Life Events Checklist | Sub-threshold for PTSD but nightmare content replicates themes |
| HRV during REM | Overnight ECG monitoring (research setting) | Reduced HRV during REM periods relative to NREM |

**Entropy Threshold**

| State | Criteria | Biological Markers |
|---|---|---|
| Protected Phenotype | Nightmares <1/month, no awakening distress, no daytime impairment | Normal REM density; intact fear extinction |
| Transition Zone | 1–3 nightmares/month, moderate awakening distress, some daytime anxiety | Elevated phasic REM activity; mild autonomic activation |
| Somatic Debt | ≥1 nightmare/week, severe awakening distress, sleep avoidance, daytime impairment | Reduced slow-wave sleep; REM fragmentation; elevated resting sympathetic tone |

**Biological Bill of Rights**

1. The right to distinguish idiopathic nightmares (narrative variety, lifelong) from PTSD nightmares (replicative, trauma-coincident onset).
2. The right to exclude REM rebound from antidepressant withdrawal or alcohol cessation before treating the nightmare as a primary dysfunction.
3. The right to imagery rehearsal therapy as first-line before pharmacological suppression of REM (prazosin, clonidine), which may interfere with emotional memory processing.
4. The right to sleep hygiene optimization (avoid REM-suppressant rebound, regular sleep schedule) before accepting lifelong pharmacotherapy.

### 12.10 REM Atonia Failure Phenotype (formerly Rapid Eye Movement Sleep Behavior Disorder 327.42 / G47.52)

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Progressive degeneration or dysfunction of brainstem circuits (sublaterodorsal nucleus, magnocellular reticular formation) that normally inhibit spinal motor neurons during REM sleep. Failure of REM-atonia allows the motor cortex to execute dream content via intact corticospinal pathways, producing dream-enactment behavior. This represents a prodromal synucleinopathy: α-synuclein deposition in brainstem precedes Parkinson's disease or Lewy body dementia by years to decades.

**Somatic Architecture Table**

| DSM-5 Criterion | Allostatic Interpretation | Somatic Origin |
|---|---|---|
| A: Vocalization/complex motor behavior during sleep | Loss of glycine/GABA-mediated motor neuron hyperpolarization during REM | Sublaterodorsal nucleus degeneration; impaired pontine-medullary inhibition |
| B: Arises from REM (>90 min after sleep onset, later portion of night) | Temporal correlation with REM periods; muscle tone suppression fails | Preserved cortical REM but absent brainstem atonia signal |
| C: Alert on awakening (not confused) | Cortical REM maintained normally; no NREM dissociation | Intact arousal systems; patient recalls dream content matching behavior |
| D1: REM without atonia on PSG | Persistence of chin/limb EMG tone during REM beyond physiological limits | Sustained submental EMG >1 μV or excessive phasic twitching |
| D2: Known synucleinopathy | REM behavior as biomarker of α-synuclein pathology | Later development of Parkinson's disease, MSA, or LBD in >50% of cases |

**Forensic Proxy Markers**

| Proxy | Palpation/Measurement Protocol | Pathological Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| REM without atonia (PSG) | Submental and anterior tibialis EMG during REM | Excessive tonic (>1 μV) or phasic (bursts >0.1 s) EMG activity in >18% of REM epochs |
| Bed partner report of dream enactment | Structured interview: punching, kicking, sitting up, or falling out of bed | Any episode in past month |
| Age at onset | Patient age when first dream-enactment behavior observed | >50 years (idiopathic); younger suggests narcolepsy or medication-induced |
| Olfactory testing (UPSIT) | University of Pennsylvania Smell Identification Test | <25th percentile (anosmia/hyposmia as early synucleinopathy marker) |

**Entropy Threshold**

| State | Criteria | Biological Markers |
|---|---|---|
| Protected Phenotype | No dream enactment, normal REM atonia | Normal olfaction; no neurodegeneration risk factors |
| Transition Zone | Isolated REM without atonia (no clinical behavior), mild hyposmia | EMG excess <30% of REM; UPSIT 15–25th percentile |
| Somatic Debt | Clinical RBD with injury, REM atonia loss >30% of REM, hyposmia | Motor Parkinson signs (bradykinesia, rigidity, tremor); MCI; abnormal DaTscan |

**Biological Bill of Rights**

1. The right to polysomnographic confirmation (REM without atonia) before accepting the diagnosis, distinguishing from NREM parasomnias and nocturnal seizures.
2. The right to informed disclosure that idiopathic RBD carries a >50% 10-year risk of synucleinopathy conversion, with annual neurological follow-up.
3. The right to environmental safety optimization (bed rails, floor padding, sharp removal) as first-line adaptation before pharmacotherapy.
4. The right to exclude medication-induced causes (SSRI, SNRI, beta-blocker) before accepting the phenotype as neurodegenerative.

### 12.11 Sensorimotor Hyperarousal Phenotype (formerly Restless Legs Syndrome 333.94 / G25.81)

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Dysfunctional central dopaminergic signaling in the diencephalic A11 nucleus and impaired iron transport across the blood-brain barrier, resulting in inappropriate sensorimotor cortex hyperexcitability at rest. Iron is a cofactor for tyrosine hydroxylase; deficiency reduces dopamine synthesis, disinhibiting spinal and cortical circuits that generate the urge-to-move signal. The circadian worsening (evening/night) reflects the normal diurnal trough of dopamine coupled with iron-dependent enzymatic dysfunction.

**Somatic Architecture Table**

| DSM-5 Criterion | Allostatic Interpretation | Somatic Origin |
|---|---|---|
| A1: Urge to move begins/worsens at rest | Reduced dopaminergic inhibition of sensorimotor cortex and spinal circuits at low-activity states; sensory afferent gain increased | Striatal D2 receptor dysfunction; impaired iron-dependent dopamine synthesis |
| A2: Relief by movement | Movement activates proprioceptive feedback that resets sensorimotor gating and increases spinal inhibitory interneuron firing | Cortical reafference from muscle/joint movement overrides spontaneous dysesthesias |
| A3: Worse in evening/night | Circadian trough of dopamine and iron availability at night; melatonin may further inhibit dopamine | Diurnal variation in serum iron and central dopamine; peak symptoms at 00:00–04:00 |
| B: ≥3x/week for ≥3 months | Chronic sensitization of the sensorimotor loop; genetic susceptibility (MEIS1, BTBD9, MAP2K5) | Cumulative sleep fragmentation; elevated sympathetic tone from sleep disruption |
| D–E: Exclusion of mimickers | Distinguish from akathisia, leg cramps, positional discomfort, neuropathy | Absence of medication-induced akathisia; no peripheral neuropathy on exam |

**Forensic Proxy Markers**

| Proxy | Palpation/Measurement Protocol | Pathological Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Serum ferritin | Venipuncture | <75 ng/mL (absolute iron deficiency); <50 ng/mL strongly associated with RLS severity |
| Periodic limb movements (PLMS) index | Overnight PSG or actigraphy with leg EMG | >15 PLMS/hour (adults); >5/hour (children) |
| Suggested immobilization test (SIT) | Patient sits with legs extended for 60 min; rate discomfort/urge to move at 5-min intervals | >40 on Visual Analog Scale by end of 60 min |
| Family history of RLS | First-degree relative with RLS | Positive predictive value >70% for genetic RLS (MEIS1/BTBD9 variants) |

**Entropy Threshold**

| State | Criteria | Biological Markers |
|---|---|---|
| Protected Phenotype | No leg restlessness, normal sleep initiation, ferritin >100 ng/mL | No PLMS; normal iron metabolism |
| Transition Zone | Occasional RLS symptoms (<2x/week), mild sleep disruption, ferritin 50–75 ng/mL | PLMS 5–15/hr; responds to iron supplementation |
| Somatic Debt | RLS ≥3x/week, significant sleep-onset delay, ferritin <50 ng/mL, PLMS >15/hr | Genetic risk variants (MEIS1/BTBD9); augmented on dopamine agonists; secondary iron deficiency from blood loss or malabsorption |

**Biological Bill of Rights**

1. The right to serum ferritin testing before any pharmacological intervention, with iron repletion as first-line when ferritin <75 ng/mL.
2. The right to distinguish primary RLS (genetic, age of onset <45, slow progression) from secondary RLS (iron deficiency, uremia, pregnancy, neuropathic).
3. The right to avoid dopamine agonist augmentation (worsening with earlier onset, increased intensity) by using alpha-2-delta ligands (gabapentin, pregabalin) as first-line for moderate-severe cases.
4. The right to exclude medication-induced akathisia (antipsychotics, antidepressants) and peripheral neuropathy before accepting the phenotype as idiopathic.

### 12.12 Chemically-Induced Sleep Disruption Phenotype (formerly Substance/Medication-Induced Sleep Disorder)

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Pharmacological interference with neurotransmitter systems governing sleep-wake regulation: substances alter the balance between sleep-promoting (GABA, adenosine, melatonin) and wake-promoting (orexin, histamine, norepinephrine, dopamine) systems. Acute intoxication disrupts sleep architecture (REM/NREM balance, sleep continuity); chronic use produces tolerance and withdrawal-related REM rebound; and many medications cause central sleep apnea (opioids) or RLS (antidepressants) via brainstem and sensorimotor pathway effects.

**Somatic Architecture Table**

| DSM-5 Criterion | Allostatic Interpretation | Somatic Origin |
|---|---|---|
| A: Prominent sleep disturbance | Substance directly alters neurotransmitter concentrations at receptors governing sleep-wake state transitions | Pharmacodynamics at GABA-A (alcohol, BZDs), adenosine (caffeine), orexin/histamine (stimulants), mu-opioid (opioids), CB1 (cannabinoids) receptors |
| B1: Onset with intoxication/withdrawal | Temporal coupling between substance exposure and sleep disruption | Drug half-life, receptor kinetics, and withdrawal timeline determine onset |
| B2: Substance known to produce sleep symptoms | Established pharmacological mechanism for sleep disruption | Dose-dependent effects on sleep architecture (REM suppression, NREM fragmentation, respiratory depression) |
| C: Not better explained by independent phenotype | Sleep disruption follows substance use course, resolves with cessation | Symptom timeline: precedes use? persists >4 weeks after cessation? |
| Types: Insomnia, daytime sleepiness, parasomnia, mixed | Substance-specific effects on sleep-wake regulatory circuits | Alcohol: early NREM enhancement then later disruption; stimulants: prolonged latency; opioids: central apnea |

**Forensic Proxy Markers**

| Proxy | Palpation/Measurement Protocol | Pathological Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Substance use timeline (TLFB) | Timeline follow-back interview for 30 days | Temporal correlation of sleep disturbance with substance use patterns |
| Urine toxicology | Multipanel drug screen | Positive for relevant substance class |
| Polysomnography for specific agents | PSG during substance use vs. abstinence | Apnea-hypopnea index change >5/hr (opioids); REM latency change >30 min |
| Smoking status | Self-report, exhaled CO test | >10 cigarettes/day (tobacco-induced sleep fragmentation) |

**Entropy Threshold**

| State | Criteria | Biological Markers |
|---|---|---|
| Protected Phenotype | No substance use, or use without sleep disturbance | Normal sleep architecture on PSG |
| Transition Zone | Occasional substance-related sleep disruption, resolves within 1 week of cessation | Mild REM rebound, transient insomnia during withdrawal |
| Somatic Debt | Chronic substance-induced sleep disruption persists >4 weeks after cessation, or severe during use | PSG abnormalities: reduced SWS, REM suppression, increased central apneas, PLMS >15/hr |

**Biological Bill of Rights**

1. The right to a 4-week abstinence trial with sleep monitoring before attributing the sleep dysfunction to a non-substance phenotype.
2. The right to sleep architecture assessment (PSG) when the substance cannot be discontinued, to quantify harm.
3. The right to distinguish medication-induced from disorder-coincident sleep disruption by temporal correlation over multiple on-off cycles.
4. The right to expect that medications prescribed for sleep (hypnotics) or mental health may themselves cause iatrogenic sleep disruption, and to have this possibility disclosed at prescription.

### 12.13 Subthreshold Sleep-Onset Disruption Phenotype (formerly Other Specified Insomnia Disorder 780.52 / G47.09)

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Incomplete arousal/sleep architecture phenotype that fails to meet full insomnia criteria in duration, frequency, or symptom set, but carries sufficient allostatic load to cause functional impairment. Represents a transitional state between acute sleep disruption (situational insomnia) and full consolidation of the insomnia phenotype, or a partial expression in which not all symptom domains are affected.

**Somatic Architecture Table**

| DSM-5 Criterion | Allostatic Interpretation | Somatic Origin |
|---|---|---|
| Brief insomnia (<3 months) | Acute allostatic response to precipitant that has not yet consolidated into conditioned arousal | Elevated cortisol/CRH from stressor that remits with stressor resolution |
| Nonrestorative sleep only | Isolated failure of sleep quality without latency/maintenance involvement | Reduced delta power or REM density; normal sleep continuity |
| Other specified reason | Partial phenotype expression not fitting criteria above | Variable somatic origin defined by the specified reason |

**Forensic Proxy Markers**

| Proxy | Palpation/Measurement Protocol | Pathological Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Sleep diary (7 days) | Actigraphy or diary: sleep latency, WASO, total sleep time | Fails at least one but not all insomnia criteria |
| Stressor timeline | Life events inventory correlated with insomnia onset | Temporal correlation with precipitating event |
| Sleep quality index (PSQI) | Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index | Global score >5 but insufficient duration/frequency for full insomnia |

**Entropy Threshold**

| State | Criteria | Biological Markers |
|---|---|---|
| Protected Phenotype | Transient sleep disruption <1 week, resolves without intervention | Normal stress recovery |
| Transition Zone | 1 week – 3 months of sleep disruption, intervention-responsive | Elevated evening cortisol, reversible |
| Somatic Debt | Nonrestorative sleep >3 months, or other persistent partial phenotype | Objective sleep disruption disproportionate to subjective complaint |

**Biological Bill of Rights**

1. The right to distinguish acute insomnia (self-limited, stressor-related) from emerging chronic insomnia via 2-week monitoring.
2. The right to designate the specific reason for subthreshold presentation to guide targeted intervention.
3. The right to behavioral intervention (stimulus control, sleep restriction) without automatic progression to pharmacotherapy.
4. The right to re-evaluation at 3 months to determine if the phenotype has consolidated or remitted.

### 12.15 Subthreshold Excessive Sleep Drive Phenotype (formerly Other Specified Hypersomnolence Disorder 780.54 / G47.19)

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Excessive sleep propensity that causes functional impairment but does not meet full hypersomnolence criteria, typically in duration (acute/subacute) or in specific phenotype expression (e.g., Kleine-Levin syndrome with episodic hypersomnia). Represents incomplete expression of the sleep drive dysregulation phenotype.

**Somatic Architecture Table**

| DSM-5 Criterion | Allostatic Interpretation | Somatic Origin |
|---|---|---|
| Symptoms characteristic of hypersomnolence | Excessive sleep drive of uncertain duration or episodic pattern | May involve transient orexinergic dysfunction (Kleine-Levin), post-viral, or post-traumatic |
| Does not meet full criteria | Duration <3 months or atypical expression | Episodic, recurrent, or situational sleep drive without consolidation |

**Forensic Proxy Markers**

| Proxy | Palpation/Measurement Protocol | Pathological Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Sleep diary + actigraphy (14 days) | Extended sleep and nap pattern monitoring | Prolonged sleep time but <3 months duration or episodic course |
| MSLT (if indicated) | Mean sleep latency across 4–5 naps | Variable, may show <8 min but with atypical presentation |

**Entropy Threshold**

| State | Criteria | Biological Markers |
|---|---|---|
| Protected Phenotype | Transient sleep need increase, resolves spontaneously | Normal arousal regulation |
| Transition Zone | Subacute hypersomnia (1–3 months), episodic pattern, or post-viral | MSLT variable; responds to sleep extension |
| Somatic Debt | Episodic (Kleine-Levin variant) with severe functional impairment | Episodic hypometabolism on SPECT; prolonged episodes >1 week |

**Biological Bill of Rights**

1. The right to specify the reason for subthreshold presentation (duration, episodic course, or atypical features).
2. The right to distinguish post-viral hypersomnia from narcolepsy via CSF hypocretin-1 and HLA typing.
3. The right to trial of wake-promoting agents only after objective daytime sleepiness is demonstrated.
4. The right to periodic reassessment for consolidation into full hypersomnolence phenotype.

### 12.17 Subthreshold Circadian or Parasomnia Phenotype (formerly Other Specified Sleep-Wake Disorder 780.59 / G47.8)
