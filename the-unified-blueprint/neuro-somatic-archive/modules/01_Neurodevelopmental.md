# Module 01 — Neurodevelopmental Ontogenies

The following phenotypes address disruptions in central nervous system development during critical ontogenetic windows — from neuronal migration and synaptic pruning to myelination and functional network specialization. Each entry reinterprets DSM-5 neurodevelopmental disorders as somatic allostatic states with identifiable biological origins.

### 1. Intellectual Phenotype (formerly Intellectual Disability /

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Reduced synaptic pruning efficiency and/or aberrant neuronal migration during corticogenesis, producing diminished cortico-cortical integration capacity. Resultant global reduction in neural signal-to-noise ratio within prefrontal, temporal, and parietal association cortices. Etiologies encompass copy number variants, inborn errors of metabolism, hypoxicischemic injury, toxin-mediated synaptopathy, and chronic social deprivation-induced glucocorticoid neurotoxicity.

### 5. Fluency Ontogeny — Stuttering Phenotype (formerly ChildhoodOnset Fluency Disorder [315.35 / F80.81])

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Basal ganglia-thalamocortical loop dysrhythmia affecting the left putamen and supplementary motor area, producing impaired initiation timing of the speech motor program. Reduced dopamine D2 receptor density in the striatum diminishes the ability to gate competing motor plans, producing syllable repetitions and blocks. The supplementary motor area fails to generate a sufficiently strong preparatory potential (Bereitschaftspotential), causing initiation failure at phoneme onset. Cortical asymmetry abnormality — right > left activation during speech — indicates missing left-hemisphere dominance for motor speech sequencing.

### 6. Social Pragmatic Phenotype (formerly Social

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Underconnectivity of the fronto-temporo-parietal social cognition network — specifically the right temporoparietal junction (mentalizing), the medial prefrontal cortex (social inference), and the posterior superior temporal sulcus (biological motion/eye gaze processing). Unlike autism, there is no involvement of the subcortical repetitive-behavior circuit (striatum, thalamus). The deficit is confined to the “social brain” network, sparing the motor programming and sensory gating systems.

### 7. Autism Spectrum Phenotype (formerly Autism Spectrum

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Polygenic neurodevelopmental disconnection syndrome characterized by (1) altered excitation/inhibition ratio in cortical microcircuitry (GABAergic interneuron migration failure, NMDA receptor subunit imbalance), (2) aberrant long-range white matter connectivity (reduced corpus callosum, superior longitudinal fasciculus, and fronto-occipital fasciculus integrity), (3) local hyperconnectivity in posterior cortical regions with reduced fronto-posterior integration, and (4) striatal enlargement with altered dopamine signaling producing repetitive-motor-circuit capture. The combined pathology produces a brain optimized for local detail processing at the expense of global integration, with impaired social reward signaling due to reduced mesolimbic dopamine response to social stimuli.

### 8. Attention-Drive Regulation Phenotype (formerly AttentionDeficit/Hyperactivity Disorder [314.0x / F90.x])

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Hypofunction of the catecholaminergic fronto-striatal-cerebellar circuits. Reduced dopaminergic (D1/D5) signaling in prefrontal cortex impairs working memory buffer maintenance and attentional set maintenance. Reduced noradrenergic signaling from locus coeruleus impairs phasic alerting and signal-to-noise enhancement. Striatal dopamine transporter (DAT) overexpression produces rapid clearance of synaptic dopamine, reducing the temporal window for reward processing and producing a steeper delay-discounting curve (preference for immediate small rewards over delayed larger rewards). Cerebellar hypoperfusion impairs temporal prediction of events. Total brain volume reduced by ~3–5% on average, with specific reduction in right caudate, prefrontal cortex, and cerebellar vermis. The condition represents a regulatory failure of the temporal dynamics of attention — the brain defaults to exploratory (distractible) mode because sustained-attention mode is metabolically too costly in the context of reduced prefrontal efficiency.

### 9. Specific Learning Phenotype (formerly Specific Learning Disorder

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Focal disruption of the cortical networks specialized for specific academic skills. For reading (dyslexia): reduced functional specialization of the left ventral occipitotemporal “visual word form area” due to impaired neuronal competition and selective pruning. Phonological processing deficit originates in reduced left planum temporale asymmetry and impaired connectivity from the auditory association cortex to the inferior frontal gyrus. For mathematics (dyscalculia): impaired number-space mapping in the bilateral intraparietal sulcus — the region that treats numbers on a spatial continuum. Children with dyscalculia fail to develop the typical right-IPS precision for quantity estimation. For written expression: impaired graphomotor output integration across the exner area (left premotor cortex), visual word form area, and prefrontal executive networks. All specific learning phenotypes share a common biological feature: a circumscribed, focal network underdevelopment in the context of otherwise intact general cognitive ability.

### 10. Motor Coordination Phenotype (formerly Developmental

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Cerebellar-cortical connectivity failure, specifically reduced P-cell output from cerebellar lobules IV–VI and Crus I/II resulting in impaired feedforward motor prediction. The internal model of expected sensory consequences of motor commands is imprecise, requiring the motor system to rely on slow, feedback-based correction rather than rapid feedforward execution. This manifests as clumsy, slow, error-prone movement. The mirror neuron system (inferior parietal lobule, ventral premotor cortex) shows reduced task-based activation and reduced connectivity to the cerebellum. The condition represents a failure of the cerebellar “forward model” — the brain cannot accurately predict the outcome of its motor commands, so it must wait for sensory feedback, producing delayed, imprecise movement.

### 11. Stereotypic Movement Phenotype (formerly Stereotypic

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Striatal motor habit-circuit hyperexcitability. The cortico-striato-thalamocortical loop that controls voluntary movement selection fails to properly inhibit prepotent motor patterns. Specifically, the indirect pathway (which suppresses unwanted movements) is underactive relative to the direct pathway (which facilitates selected movements). This produces “motor capture” — repetitive, rhythmic, seemingly driven movements that the individual cannot suppress without external sensory interruption. The movements may be self-stimulatory (providing sensory reinforcement in understimulated environments) or self-regulatory (reducing anxiety-induced autonomic arousal). Self-injurious forms involve additional recruitment of the periaqueductal gray and endogenous opioid circuits, potentially producing analgesia that reinforces the behavior.

### 12. Tourette’s Phenotype (formerly Tourette’s Disorder [307.23 /

**Allostatic Load Profile**

Striatal disinhibition, characterized by reduced number and altered morphology of parvalbumin-positive GABAergic interneurons in the caudate and putamen, leading to failed inhibition of unwanted motor and vocal programs. The premonitory urge that precedes each tic represents sensory hyperexcitability in the supplementary motor area and insula — these regions become pathologically aware of subthreshold motor preparation signals that are normally filtered out. Dopamine dysregulation (presynaptic excess, D2 receptor hypersensitivity) lowers the threshold for striatal output, releasing tics via the direct pathway. The sensorimotor cortex and putamen show reduced volume on MRI, and there is reduced structural connectivity (FA) in the cortico-striato-thalamo-cortical tracts. The longitudinal course — peaking in preadolescence with decline thereafter — mirrors the developmental trajectory of dopamine receptor expression.

### 14. Provisional Tic Phenotype (formerly Provisional Tic Disorder

**Allostatic Load Profile**

The neurochemistry is unstable but self-correcting in the majority of cases. No fixed striatal pathology is present — the tics resolve as the dopamine receptor density undergoes developmental normalization. The <1-year duration is the defining feature separating this developmental phenomenon from a fixed pathology. This may represent the upper end of the normal distribution of motor inhibition development rather than a disorder.
