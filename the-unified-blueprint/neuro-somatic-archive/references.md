## References

The following works provide the scientific, clinical, and philosophical foundations for the Neuro-Somatic Archive. Each is listed with a brief note on its specific contribution to the framework.

---

Sterling, P. & Eyer, J. (1988). "Allostasis: A New Paradigm to Explain Arousal Pathology." *In: Fisher, S. & Reason, J. (eds.) Handbook of Life Stress, Cognition and Health*. Wiley.

> The original formulation of allostasis as an alternative to homeostasis. Sterling and Eyer demonstrated that the autonomic nervous system does not defend a single set point but continuously adjusts regulatory targets based on environmental demands. This paper provides the theoretical skeleton for the Archive's allostatic load model and its rejection of the homeostatic baseline assumption.

McEwen, B.S. (1998). "Protective and Damaging Effects of Stress Mediators." *New England Journal of Medicine*, 338(3): 171–179.

> McEwen's landmark paper introduced the concept of allostatic load as the "wear and tear" resulting from chronic stress system activation. This is the direct empirical foundation for the Archive's entropy threshold classification — the distinction between adaptive allostasis (protective) and cumulative load (damaging) maps directly onto the Protected/Transition/Somatic Debt system.

Porges, S.W. (2011). *The Polyvagal Theory: Neurophysiological Foundations of Emotions, Attachment, Communication, and Self-Regulation*. Norton.

> Porges's polyvagal theory provides the autonomic architecture that the Archive uses to map "emotional" and "behavioral" states to specific vagal nerve activation patterns. The ventral vagal (social engagement), sympathetic (fight/flight), and dorsal vagal (shutdown) hierarchy maps directly onto the Archive's treatment of dissociative, hyperarousal, and collapse phenotypes as biologically structured rather than psychologically chosen.

Damasio, A. (1994). *Descartes' Error: Emotion, Reason, and the Human Brain*. Putnam.

> Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis — that emotional states are fundamentally bodily states interpreted by the brain — provides the philosophical warrant for the Archive's core claim: that "mental" disorders are somatic phenomena. If emotion is the readout of interoceptive signals, then psychiatric classification must begin with the body, not the mind. The Archive operationalizes this principle through its forensic proxy protocol.

Sapolsky, R.M. (2004). *Why Zebras Don't Get Ulcers*, 3rd ed. Holt.

> Sapolsky's accessible account of stress physiology — particularly the distinction between acute adaptive stress responses and the damage caused by chronic activation — informs the Archive's treatment of allostatic load as a cumulative variable. The zebra metaphor (acute stress response saves life; chronic activation destroys it) is embedded in the Archive's refusal to pathologize acute adaptive responses while aggressively intervening in chronic states.

American Psychiatric Association (2013). *Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders*, 5th ed. APA.

> The DSM-5 is the Archive's primary historical interlocutor — the document whose categorical system, subjective-report epistemology, and pathology-first framework the Archive replaces. Every Archive phenotype includes a cross-reference to its DSM-5 equivalent or equivalents. The DSM-5 is not rejected; it is *reclassified* as historical data rather than clinical truth.

American Psychiatric Association (2022). *Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders*, 5th ed., Text Revision (DSM-5-TR). APA.

> The DSM-5-TR is referenced for its diagnostic criteria updates and its inclusion of prolonged grief disorder and other revisions. The Archive engages with the DSM-5-TR as the most current version of the system it supersedes, ensuring that all phenotype cross-references are accurate to the latest edition.

Friston, K. (2010). "The free-energy principle: a unified brain theory?" *Nature Reviews Neuroscience*, 11(2): 127–138.

> Friston's free-energy principle — that biological systems minimize surprise by updating internal models of the world — provides the computational framework for the Archive's treatment of "delusions," "hallucinations," and "distorted thinking" as Bayes-optimal inference under conditions of high uncertainty or unreliable sensory data. If the brain is a prediction engine, then "psychotic" phenotypes represent prediction errors in systems operating under extreme prior weightings or degraded sensory evidence.

Seth, A.K. (2013). "Interoceptive inference, emotion, and the embodied self." *Trends in Cognitive Sciences*, 17(11): 565–573.

> Seth extends Friston's free-energy principle to interoception — the brain's inference about the internal state of the body. This provides the mechanism by which "emotional" states and "mood" phenotypes are grounded in bodily sensing. The Archive's treatment of depression, anxiety, and dissociative states as interoceptive inference failures (rather than chemical imbalances) is directly derived from this work.

## Supplementary References

Van der Kolk, B.A. (2014). *The Body Keeps the Score: Brain, Mind, and Body in the Healing of Trauma*. Viking.

> Clinical evidence for the somatic encoding of traumatic experience. Supports the Archive's claim that trauma phenotypes must be assessed through biological markers, not narrative recall.

Levine, P.A. (1997). *Waking the Tiger: Healing Trauma*. North Atlantic Books.

> Somatic-experiential model of trauma as incomplete biological responses. Informs the Archive's treatment of chronic hyperarousal and dissociative states as interrupted action sequences stored in the body.

Bernardi, L., et al. (1996). "Effect of rosary prayer and yoga mantras on autonomic cardiovascular rhythms." *BMJ*, 312: 1443–1446.

> Demonstrates that rhythmic breathing practices produce measurable entrainment of cardiovascular oscillations at 6 cycles/minute — a frequency that optimizes baroreflex sensitivity. Supports the Archive's inclusion of respiratory-based proxies and interventions.

Schulkin, J. (2011). *Adaptation and Well-Being: Social Allostasis*. Cambridge University Press.

> Extends allostatic load theory into the social domain, arguing that social structures themselves are allostatic regulatory mechanisms. Provides theoretical support for the Archive's claim that environmental restructuring is a primary intervention modality.
