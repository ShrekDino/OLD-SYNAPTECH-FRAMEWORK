## SECTION 1: Economics of Assistive vs. Enhancement Neurotechnology Markets

### Academic Sources

**1. Yuste, R., Goering, S., et al. (2017). "Four ethical priorities for neurotechnologies and AI." Nature, 551, 159-163.**
DOI: 10.1038/551159a | Citations: 600+
Key takeaway: Identifies therapy-enhancement boundary blurring. The same device can move between assistive (medical device regulated) and enhancement (consumer market) regulatory regimes.

**2. Goering, S., Klein, E., Sullivan, L.S., Wexler, A., & Aguera y Arcas, B. (2021). "Recommendations for Responsible Development and Application of Neurotechnologies." Neuroethics, 14, 149-160.**
DOI: 10.1007/s12152-021-09468-6 | Citations: 208
Key takeaway: Market forces push toward enhancement applications (larger consumer markets, higher margins). Assistive market serves populations with less economic power.

**3. Farah, M.J. (2015). "An Ethics Toolbox for Neurotechnology." Neuron, 86(1), 34-37.**
DOI: 10.1016/j.neuron.2015.03.038 | Citations: 54
Key takeaway: Same technology has different economics depending on assistive vs. enhancement classification. Insurance reimbursement is the single largest economic driver.

**4. Bostrom, N. (2003). "Human Genetic Enhancements: A Transhumanist Perspective." The Journal of Value Inquiry, 37, 493-506.**
DOI: 10.1023/b:inqu.0000019037.67783.d5 | Citations: 381
Key takeaway: Therapy-enhancement distinction is economically irrational. If a technology produces welfare gains, restricting it to therapeutic uses is inefficient.

**5. McIntosh, E. (2011). "Perspective on the economic evaluation of deep brain stimulation." Frontiers in Integrative Neuroscience, 5:19.**
DOI: 10.3389/fnint.2011.00019 | Citations: 45
Key takeaway: DBS costs 5K-00K per patient. Cost-effectiveness 0K-00K/QALY. Neurotechnology is expensive enough to create access barriers.

**6. Moon, W. et al. (2017). "Cost-effectiveness of DBS for treatment-resistant OCD." Medicine, 96(27), e7397.**
DOI: 10.1097/md.0000000000007397 | Citations: 29
Key takeaway: 1,490/QALY for DBS-OCD in Korea. Methodological model for evaluating neuroprosthetics economically.

**7. Ooms, P. et al. (2017). "Cost-effectiveness of DBS vs. treatment as usual for OCD." Brain Stimulation, 10(4), 836-842.**
DOI: 10.1016/j.brs.2017.04.120 | Citations: 39
Key takeaway: 82,400 EUR/QALY for DBS-OCD. Cost-effective compared to treatment-as-usual.

## SECTION 2: Why Neuro-Orthotics Cannot Be Treated as Mere Property/Collateral

**8. Vold, K. & Liao, X. (2024). "Neuroprosthetics, Extended Cognition, and the Problem of Ownership." Techno:Phil, 3, 47-68.**
DOI: 10.1007/978-3-662-68362-0_3 | Citations: 3
Key takeaway: Extended mind theory applied to neuroprosthetics. If integrated into the user cognitive system, device constitutes part of self. Cannot be treated as ordinary property. Collateral/repossession creates ethical impossibility.

**9. Naufel, S. & Klein, E. (2019). "BCI researcher perspectives on neural data ownership and privacy." Journal of Neural Engineering, 16(6), 066045.**
DOI: 10.1088/1741-2552/ab5b7f | Citations: 41
Key takeaway: 75%+ of BCI researchers agree neural data is qualitatively different from other biometric data - constitutes neural correlates of thought, identity, and intention.

**10. Ligthart, S., Ienca, M., et al. (2023). "Minding Rights: Mapping Ethical and Legal Foundations of Neurorights." Cambridge Quarterly of Healthcare Ethics, 32(4), 554-568.**
DOI: 10.1017/s0963180123000245 | Citations: 114
Key takeaway: Right to equal access to mental augmentation directly provides economic argument against market-based exclusion. If augmentation is a right, devices cannot be repossession commodities.

**11. Borban, D. et al. (2021). "NeuroRight to Equal Access to Mental Augmentation." Revista Iberoamericana de Bioetica, 16, 1-15.**
DOI: 10.14422/rib.i16.y2021.006 | Citations: 10
Key takeaway: Mental augmentation technologies constitute a fundamental human right. Devices must be publicly provided and cannot be subject to repossession or collateralization.

**12. Solinsky, R. & Sullivan, L.S. (2018). "Ethical Issues Surrounding Neuroprostheses for SCI Patients." PMandR, 10(9), S311-S318.**
DOI: 10.1016/j.pmrj.2018.05.020 | Citations: 2
Key takeaway: Functionally integrated neuroprostheses removal constitutes harm equivalent to removing a limb. Repossession for non-payment inflicts bodily harm.

## SECTION 3: Cost-Benefit Analyses of Neural Augmentation

**13. Dunlop, M. & Savulescu, J. (2014). "Distributive justice and cognitive enhancement in lower, normal intelligence." Monash Bioethics Review, 32, 193-209.**
DOI: 10.1007/s40592-014-0014-7 | Citations: 21
Key takeaway: Greatest marginal benefit from raising floor, not ceiling. Economic justice argument for universal access.

**14. Buchanan, A. (2011). "Cognitive enhancement and education." Theory and Research in Education, 9(2), 145-162.**
DOI: 10.1177/1477878511409623 | Citations: 75
Key takeaway: Enhancement tech parallels education as human capital investment. If education is a public good, neural augmentation should be too.

**15. Dubljevic, V. (2012). "Toward a Legitimate Public Policy on Cognition-Enhancement Drugs." AJOB Neuroscience, 3(3), 29-42.**
DOI: 10.1080/21507740.2012.700681 | Citations: 28
Key takeaway: Prohibition of safe enhancement technologies is economically inefficient and creates black markets.

## SECTION 4: Wealth Inequality Implications

**16. Veit, W. (2018). "Enhancement technologies and inequality." PhilSci-Archive.**
DOI: 10.13140/rg.2.2.21932.08326 | Citations: 6
Key takeaway: Enhancement technologies are amplifier technologies. Outcome depends on distribution mechanism: private purchase compounds inequality; public provision could reduce it.

**17. Swindells, F. (2014). "Economic Inequality and Human Enhancement Technology."**
Citations: 5
Key takeaway: Unequal access creates winner-take-all dynamics. Wealthy augment productivity, widening wage gaps and reducing mobility.

**18. Munoz, J.M. & Borban, D. (2023). "Equal access to mental augmentation: Should it be a fundamental right?" Brain Stimulation, 16(3), 894-895.**
DOI: 10.1016/j.brs.2023.05.003 | Citations: 14
Key takeaway: Without public provision, enhancement creates neuro-aristocracy - permanent cognitive class divide making social mobility impossible.

**19. Ienca, M. (2021). "On Neurorights." Frontiers in Human Neuroscience, 15:701258.**
DOI: 10.3389/fnhum.2021.701258 | Citations: 145
Key takeaway: Neurorights framework requires states to ensure equitable distribution of neurotechnologies.

**20. Hertz, N. (2022). "Neurorights - Do we Need New Human Rights?" Neuroethics, 15:31.**
DOI: 10.1007/s12152-022-09511-0 | Citations: 90
Key takeaway: Neurotechnologies could create cognitive classes undermining democratic equality.

## SECTION 5: Economic Arguments in Transhumanist Rights Literature

**21. Hughes, J. (2004). Citizen Cyborg. Basic Books.**
Citations: 404+
Key takeaway: Democratic transhumanism - enhancement must be democratically controlled and universally accessible. Positive-sum if distributed equally; zero-sum if captured by elites.

**22. Bostrom, N. (2005). "In Defense of Posthuman Dignity." Bioethics, 19(3), 202-214.**
DOI: 10.1111/j.1467-8519.2005.00441.x | Citations: 500+
Key takeaway: Dignity arguments used to restrict enhancement are economically regressive.

**23. Ongun, E. (2019). "Evaluation of Transhumanist Bill of Rights." In Handbook of Research on Learning in the Age of Transhumanism. IGI Global.**
DOI: 10.4018/978-1-5225-8431-5.ch021 | Citations: 1
Key takeaway: Transhumanist Bill of Rights includes right to use enhancement tech. Constrains property-based exclusion.

**24. Calleja, C. (2018). "The bioethics of enhancement: transhumanism, disability, biopolitics." Political Theology, 19(6), 478-497.**
DOI: 10.1462317x.2018.1505374 | Citations: 55
Key takeaway: Transhumanist market logic can devalue disabled lives - fixing prioritized over accommodating.

**25. Pillay, T. (2024). "Rethinking the right to freedom of thought." International Journal of Human Rights, 28(9), 1459-1483.**
DOI: 10.1080/13642987.2024.2390442 | Citations: 2
Key takeaway: Cognitive liberty creates economic principle against prohibition of safe enhancement.

**26. Rainey, S. (2023). "Neurorights as Hohfeldian Privileges." Neuroethics, 16:27.**
DOI: 10.1007/s12152-023-09515-4 | Citations: 11
Key takeaway: Hohfeldian framework: neurorights as privileges create negative duties (non-interference), not necessarily positive duties (provision).

**27. Hall, M.C. (2018). The Bioethics of Enhancement. Rowman and Littlefield.**
Citations: 18+
Key takeaway: Transhumanist economic framing reproduces ableist logics. Bodies seen as underperforming assets.
